The Most Costly Winterizing Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

What Everyone Gets Wrong About the Water Heater Bypass Valve

Listen up, because this is where the amateurs start to pour money down the drainâ€”literally. The single most common mistake, repeated every fall, is failing to engage the water heater bypass valve. The function of this valve is simple: it routes the RV antifreeze around the water heater tank instead of letting it fill the tank.

Why is this critical? A typical six-gallon water heater, even when drained, will have residual water. To displace that six or ten gallons with RV antifreeze, youâ€™d need an exorbitant amount of the stuff. If you use the standard two-gallon method, that anti-freeze will simply dilute into the water, achieving nothing but a very expensive pink mixture that will still freeze.

Worse, the dilution was so severe that the residual water in the tank still froze solid, cracking the sacrificial anode rod mount. The total repair bill was $850, all because of a single, small valve.

The Fix:

  • Three-Valve Systems (Most Common): You have three valves behind your water heater. Close the cold water inlet and hot water outlet valves. Open the crossover valve in the middle. This creates the bypass loop.
  • Single-Valve Systems (Newer/Simpler): This is a single handle. Turn the handle 90 degrees to redirect the flow. Always confirm that when you turn on the pump, the water (or anti-freeze) is not flowing to the water heater. You should see fluid emerge from the closest hot-side faucet almost immediately.

Air Blow-Out: The PSI Threshold That Guarantees Line Damage

Using compressed air is a fast, clean way to remove the bulk of the water, but it’s a technique shrouded in bad advice and inflated ego. Stop listening to the guy at the campsite who boasts about using his 120 PSI shop compressor to “blast out” the lines. He’s not thorough; he’s just destructive.

Here is the technical reality that your plastic PEX fittings and seals cannot ignore: exceeding 40 PSI is dangerous. The seals, check valves, and various plastic fittings in your travel trailer’s plumbing system are simply not designed to withstand industrial-level pressure. While a quick blast might seem efficient, the elevated pressure can subtly fatigue plastic threads and rubber seals. You won’t see the damage now, but next spring, you’ll be chasing a constant drip behind a cabinetâ€”a leak that guarantees a mold issue.

The critical piece of equipment here is not the compressor; it’s the brass regulator you use at the city water inlet, often sold with a common quick-connect fitting. You absolutely must attach a pressure regulatorâ€”a cheap, dedicated, in-line regulatorâ€”and set the output to 30 PSI, maximum 40 PSI. If you don’t have a regulator, stop now and buy one. This is not optional.

Step-by-Step: The Correct Sequence

The blow-out method requires a specific order to ensure every drop of water is expelled. The goal isn’t brute force; it’s a patient, low-pressure sweep:

  1. Set your regulated air pressure to 30 PSI and connect it to your city water inlet.
  2. Start with the faucet furthest from the inlet (usually the bathroom shower).
  3. Open the cold water side first. Let the air blow until no water mist comes out for 30 seconds. Close it.
  4. Open the hot water side. Blow until clear for 30 seconds. Close it.
  5. Move to the next furthest faucet (e.g., kitchen sink) and repeat steps 3 and 4.
  6. Finally, don’t forget the low-point drains (open, blow, close) and the toilet flush valve (hold down until clear).

Only once the bulk of the water is removed with this gentle, sequenced blow-out should you introduce the RV antifreeze to protect the traps, seals, and water pump components.

The Proven Anti-Freeze Method: Propylene Glycol Concentration is Key

While the blow-out method is faster, the anti-freeze method offers superior protection, especially in zones classified as US Hardiness Zone 6 and below. Crucially, the quality and concentration of your RV anti-freeze (propylene glycol) is more important than the quantity you use. We test the freeze point of three common brands.

First, let’s kill the myth: RV anti-freeze is not automotive anti-freeze. Automotive coolant is ethylene glycolâ€”highly toxic and absolutely forbidden in a potable water system. If you pour that green sludge into your lines, you’ve just bought yourself a new RV plumbing system. RV anti-freeze is specifically formulated with propylene glycol, a food-grade, non-toxic alcohol. It is safe for incidental consumption, though we don’t recommend making a cocktail out of it.

The real game-changer is the temperature rating on the jug. Most brands scream “$-50^{\circ}\text{F}$ Protection!” The truly helpful number you need to focus on is the burst point, not the freeze point. The RV industry knows you’ll see $-50^{\circ}\text{F}$ and think you’re safe. However, that rating often refers to the slush pointâ€”the temperature at which ice crystals begin to form. The burst point is the temperature at which the mixture freezes solid and has enough expansive force to crack your pipes and fittings. A $-50^{\circ}\text{F}$ RV fluid may only offer a burst point of $-10^{\circ}\text{F}$ to $-20^{\circ}\text{F}$. You must know the difference and purchase a product with a reliable, documented burst point for your climate.

Using the water pump correctly is non-negotiable for this method. Ignore the freshwater tank completely. Your pump should have a dedicated siphon tube or winterization port near the inlet. Use a small length of hose to run directly from this port into your jug of RV anti-freeze. Turn the pump on, open the closest faucet (cold first, then hot) until the pink fluid runs steadily, then move on. This ensures maximum concentration where it matters most: the interior of your PEX lines.

Flushing and Protecting the Gray and Black Holding Tanks

The waste tanks are a different beast, but they still require attention when you winterize a travel trailer. Many owners simply dump a gallon of anti-freeze into an empty black tank and call it a day, which is one of the most common mistakes we see.

The anti-freeze is supposed to protect the gate valves and a small pool in the bottom of the tank to prevent odor, but pouring it into a completely dry, empty tank can actually damage your sensor probes. Those inexpensive probes are notorious for failing, and leaving them dry and exposed to pure alcohol for months accelerates their deterioration.

The essential step is lubrication. Before adding the anti-freeze, you need to properly clean and deodorize your black tank and then add protection to the gate valve seals. These seals are rubber or plastic and will dry out and crack over the winter if not maintained.

  1. Empty and Flush: Use a thorough flush/rinse cycle to clean both tanks completely.
  2. Add Lube: Pour $1$ cup of a non-toxic plumbing lubricant (specific for RV/marine applications, not motor oil!) down the toilet and open/close the black tank gate valve a few times. Do the same for the shower drain to lube the gray tank valve. This is the only way to ensure they operate properly when you de-winterize.
  3. Add Protection: Only then should you add $1$ gallon of RV anti-freeze to the black tank and $1/2$ gallon to the gray tank. This creates a protective pool around the gate valve seals and provides enough volume to prevent any residual water in the bottom from freezing solid.

This multi-step approach is what separates the expert from the amateurâ€”it ensures not just pipe integrity but also the functional longevity of your entire waste system.

Pest Control and Seal Lubrication: A Trust-First Approach

The goal here isn’t to turn your RV into a toxic chemical warfare zone, but to make it thoroughly uninviting to everything from mice to microscopic mold spores. More critically, you must address your rubber seals. These components are the number one barrier against moisture and pests, yet they are almost universally neglected.
