üí® Natural Ways to Make Bees Say “Nope”

Bees rely heavily on their sense of smell. It’s how they find flowers, talk to each other, and generally navigate the world. So, to make them move out, you need to introduce smells they absolutely hate.

1. Use Scents They Can’t Stand

You don’t need a crazy chemical spray. Head to your pantry! Bees are basically drama queens when it comes to strong scents.

  • Cinnamon Powder: Sprinkle a generous amount of cinnamon right near where the bees are entering or building. Do this every day for about a week. The super-strong, woody smell can make them feel uncomfortable enough to pack up and move.
    • Hot Take: Some beekeepers say this is total BS, but plenty of homeowners swear it worked for them. It’s worth a try since it’s cheap and harmless.
  • Citronella Candles/Oil: We use this to keep mosquitoes away, but it also works on bees. Lighting a citronella candle near the area, or spraying the oil on nearby window frames, can irritate them into leaving.
  • Peppermint Oil: Mix a few drops of peppermint essential oil with water in a spray bottle. Mist it lightly around the area. It won’t kill them, but it will definitely send the message: “You are not welcome here.”

2. The Smoke Signal

Beekeepers use smoke all the time. But not to burn the hive‚Äîthat’s a disaster waiting to happen.

When bees smell smoke, they think their home is about to catch fire. This makes them panic and start gorging on honey, getting ready to flee and start a new colony somewhere else. It also messes with the scent signals they use to communicate.

Do this ONLY if you can do it safely: Create a small amount of light, non-toxic smoke (like from burning wood chips or old cardboard) under the area. The smoke is just a gentle push to make them feel like, “Okay, time to find a new house.”

WARNING: Bees can get aggressive when they’re smoked. Do this at night when they are all inside, and keep your distance once the smoke starts.

Once you’ve gotten those little buzzers to move out, you don’t want them doing a U-turn next season.

  • Patch it Up: Use caulk or wood filler to seal every little hole. This is especially true for the hole they were just using.
  • Screen it Off: If you have vents or open chimney tops, make sure they have a proper screen that will let air through but keep the insects out.
  • Remove the Sweet Stuff: Bees are attracted to sugary things. Keep your outdoor garbage cans sealed tight, and rinse out soda cans and juice boxes before you toss them.

Look, having a bee problem is annoying, but it doesn’t have to be a death sentence for the hive. You can absolutely be a decent human, save the bees, and still rid bees from your property.

üßê Quick ID Check: Bee vs. Wasp vs. Hornet

  • Wasps and Hornets (The Mean Ones): Think glossy leather jackets. They’re skinny, sleek, and bright yellow or black. They have a narrow “waist,” which makes them look like they’re wearing tiny little belts.

Bottom line: If it’s fuzzy and chunky, it’s a bee. Be nice to it. If it’s skinny and shiny, you’re dealing with a wasp, and you can be a bit less gentle with your removal strategy.
