ü•∂ The Great Heat Blockade: Keep the Enemy Out

Your first mission is a defensive one. You need to stop the sun and the outside air from even setting foot in your room. If the heat never gets in, you don’t have to fight it later. Genius!

This is the most obvious, but also the most effective, tip. Close your blinds and curtains during the day, especially the ones that get direct sunlight. That big window is basically a solar panel, and it‚Äôs charging your room with heat.

  • Don’t just close the blinds‚Äîmake sure your actual windows and doors are closed too. This keeps the hot air outside where it belongs.
  • It might feel weird, but you are not adding heat by keeping the sun out. You’re just keeping the heat from sneaking in and making things worse.

Ditch Those Old Light Bulbs

Hot tip from the Department of Obvious: light bulbs create heat. Especially those old, twisty CFL or classic incandescent bulbs. They waste most of their energy creating heat instead of light.

Switch to LED bulbs. They use way less electricity and hardly give off any heat at all. It’s a small change, but it stops a hidden source of warmth. Plus, you‚Äôll save a little cash on your power bill too! It’s what the cool kids are doing.

This is a classic for a reason. Grab a metal bowl or a shallow pan and fill it with ice. Then, place that ice bowl in front of a fan that‚Äôs pointing toward you.

  2. Put one fan facing out of a window to push the hot air trapped inside your room right into the night.
  3. Put a second fan facing in through a window on the opposite side of the room. This sucks in the cool night air.

This creates a cross-breeze that acts like a vacuum, sucking the stale, warm air out and replacing it with cooler air. It‚Äôs a genius move for cooling down before bed.

  • Take a quick cold shower before bed. It lowers your core temperature, making it way easier to fall asleep.
  • Put your sheets (or just a pillowcase) in a plastic bag and stick them in the freezer for 30 minutes before you get into bed. You get an amazing, brief blast of cold heaven. It doesn’t last all night, but those first few minutes are priceless.

Hot take: You gotta ditch the heavy flannel and thick cotton in the summer. They trap heat like a blanket fort.
