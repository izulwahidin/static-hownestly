The Definitive Answer: How Many Grams Are In A Quarter?

Let’s skip the preamble, the confusing lingo, and the unnecessary suspense. We’re here for the definitive answer, and here it is: a quarter ounce of cannabis, commonly called a “quarter,” equals 7 grams.

There. No more guessing. No more relying on vague, stoner-math approximations that conveniently round down for the dispensary and up for you.

You came here for an informational answer, but you’re also here for a commercial one—you need to know if you’re getting your money’s worth. The cannabis industry, in its infinite wisdom, loves to hide behind a veil of Imperial measurement jargon, making simple value comparisons needlessly complicated. We’re demystifying this system immediately. We’ll show you the true metrics, the confusing rounding errors you need to watch out for, and how to spot if you’re getting fleeced, ensuring that the grams in a quarter you pay for are the grams you actually receive.

The Non-Negotiable Math: Why a Quarter Must Equal 7 Grams

Forget the fluffy strain descriptions and the artisanal packaging—when you walk into a dispensary, understanding the weight is the most important part of buying. Anything else is just marketing. Here is the simple (but often confused) division that dictates the standard purchase size for a quarter-ounce: 7 grams. If your dealer or dispensary says otherwise, they’re either mathematically challenged or trying to short you.

The Ounce Breakdown: Why We Round 28.35 Down to 28

Let’s address the pedants in the room first. The technical weight of a single ounce in the Imperial system is precisely $28.3495$ grams. It’s a messy number that’s completely irrelevant to the business of buying weed.

Why? Because the industry—from the grower to the end-user—quickly realized that forcing a metric sale based on a hyper-specific Imperial conversion is a recipe for chaos, bad math, and perpetually sticky scales. The industry standard is to round down to a clean, easy-to-divide 28 grams per ounce.

This rounding is the bedrock of your standard purchase sizes. Once the ounce is set at 28 grams, the math for a quarter-ounce becomes non-negotiable:

$$\text{Quarter Ounce} = \frac{\text{1 Ounce}}{4} = \frac{28 \text{ grams}}{4} = 7 \text{ grams}$$

If a retailer is using anything but 7 grams as the standard for a quarter-ounce, you’re not getting a quarter; you’re getting a smaller, arbitrary fraction. The 28-gram standard is the only reason the $7$-gram conversion for how many grams in a quarter is universally accepted. Any deviation is a shortcut to ripping off the customer.

Visual Confirmation: What 7 Grams Actually Looks Like

Since a scale isn’t always available at the point of sale (or after you get home), you need the experience signal of knowing what a proper quarter looks like. Because not all flower is created equal, volume can be deceptive. A quarter-ounce (7 grams) is often compared to the size of a large strawberry or, if you prefer the coin analogy, it weighs roughly the same as two U.S. quarters/coins stacked together.

However, the real key is understanding bud density. Density is the silent killer of volume estimation:

  • Fluffy, Sativa-leaning Strains: A 7-gram purchase of a looser, airier strain (like a classic Haze) might fill a small deli container. It looks impressive because it occupies more space.
  • Dense, Indica-leaning Strains: A 7-gram purchase of a strain with hard, compact buds (like a traditional Kush) will look dramatically smaller, perhaps only covering the bottom of that same container.

The volume does not matter; the weight is king. You are buying the weight of the active compounds, not the air trapped between the leaves. A good rule of thumb: A proper 7-gram quarter should feel substantial—it’s easily twice as heavy as an eighth (3.5 grams), not just a little bit more. If your 7-gram purchase doesn’t feel significantly heavier than a standard eighth, you need a second opinion (or a better scale).

Slang Decoded: ‘Quad’ and Other Names for a Quarter-Ounce

Moving past the math and the visuals, let’s nail the language. If you want to sound like you know what you’re doing, you need to understand the slang. The most common term you will hear for a quarter-ounce is a ‘Quad’ or sometimes just a ‘Q’. The term ‘quad’ is an obvious truncation of ‘quarter-ounce’ and has become the industry shorthand for the 7-gram quantity.

Other regional, less common terms include ‘Quarter,’ ‘Q.P.’ (Quarter Pound, obviously, is a huge step up, but you might hear ‘Q’ used ambiguously), and simply using the metric weight: ‘a seven.’

  • The Smart Context: While using ‘Quad’ with your regular supplier or a trusted dispensary budtender is fine—it’s fast and accepted—when dealing with a new, unfamiliar source, stick to the formal term: “I’d like a quarter-ounce, please.” This demonstrates clarity and professionalism.

The historical context is simple: a ‘quarter’ is just part of a long-standing, pre-legalization practice of dividing illicit goods into easy-to-manage, saleable fractions of a pound or an ounce. It’s a cultural term that has transcended its roots because the math (7 grams) is so simple. Using the right terminology signals that you understand the culture, not just the conversion.

The Quarter’s Place: Value, Usage, and the Cost-Efficiency Test

The quarter—that delightful $7\text{g}$ package—is often the sweet spot for the savvy consumer. It promises a significant volume that lasts longer than a quick eighth, but without the wallet-crushing, commitment-heavy price tag of a full ounce. It is, quite simply, the best value-to-volume ratio for the average user. But don’t just trust me; we’re going to put that assumption to the cold, hard test of arithmetic. Is it actually the smart buy for you? Let’s check the math that your retailer hopes you never bother to do.

Cost-Efficiency: Calculating the Real Price Per Gram

If you’re still buying two eighths when you know you need a half-quarter (and you do know that, because you’re reading this), you’re essentially lighting money on fire. The only way to truly gauge the value of any purchase is to ignore the sticker price and calculate the Price Per Gram (PPG).

The formula is painfully simple, yet criminally underused:

$$\text{Price Per Gram (PPG)} = \frac{\text{Total Cost of Purchase}}{\text{Total Grams Purchased}}$$

The difference is rarely trivial. In a competitive market, retailers offer bulk discounts precisely because they want to move more product in one transaction. Below is a look at what two eighths ($2 \times 3.5\text{g} = 7\text{g}$) will cost you compared to a single quarter ($7\text{g}$), based on typical market pricing structures.

Package Size Grams Typical Price Range Price Per Gram (PPG)
Eighth $3.5\text{g}$ $\$30 – \$50$ $\$8.57 – \$14.28$
Quarter $7\text{g}$ $\$50 – \$90$ $\$7.14 – \$12.85$
Ounce $28\text{g}$ $\$180 – \$300$ $\$6.43 – \$10.71$

The Conclusion: If you buy two eighths at the mid-range price of $\$40$ each, you’ve paid $\$80$ for $7\text{g}$. A single, mid-range quarter at $\$70$ saves you $\$10$ instantly for the exact same amount of product, lowering your PPG from $\$11.43$ to $\$10.00$. You just paid approximately 14% more to avoid the commitment of the quarter. That’s money you could have spent on better snacks. Learn the PPG, and your wallet will thank you.

How Long Will 7 Grams Last You? (The Consumption Matrix)

The question of longevity is the most practical one: you need to know if a quarter will get you to the next payday, or if you’ll be making an awkward run to the dispensary halfway through the week. The answer, of course, depends entirely on your habits, but we can break down the typical yields to give you a concrete baseline.

A $7\text{g}$ quarter can yield a significant amount, depending on your delivery method:

  • Joints: If you roll conservative $0.5\text{g}$ joints, you’ll get 14 pre-rolls. If you prefer thicker $1.0\text{g}$ joints (the type that makes polite conversation difficult), you’ll get 7.
  • Bowls/Bongs: Assuming a generous $0.25\text{g}$ per full bowl, you’re looking at 28 separate sessions.
  • Vaporizers: Vaping is the most efficient method. A typical session might use $0.15\text{g}$, yielding over 45 sessions.

Now, let’s turn those yields into real-world consumption profiles:

User Profile Consumption Rate (Approximate) Quarter ($7\text{g}$) Longevity
Light User $0.5\text{g}$ per week (Weekends only) 14 Weeks
Moderate User $0.5\text{g}$ per day (After work) 14 Days (2 Weeks)
Heavy User $1.0\text{g}$ to $1.5\text{g}$ per day 4.5 to 7 Days

For those looking to venture into home chemistry, $7\text{g}$ is also the perfect, low-commitment amount for infusions. It’s enough to easily make a small batch of high-potency butter or coconut oil—typically yielding around 1 cup of finished product. This lets you experiment with dosage and recipes without wasting an entire ounce when you inevitably overcook the first batch.

Getting Shorted: How to Protect Your Wallet (and Your Weight)

The worst feeling is getting home and realizing your quarter feels suspiciously light. It’s that moment of sinking dread when you realize you might have paid for seven grams but walked away with five. This isn’t just about an embarrassing conversation with your supplier; it’s about protecting your wallet from unnecessary shrinkage. Here is the direct, no-nonsense action plan to ensure you are always getting the full 7 grams (or $7.087$ grams, if we’re being pedantic).

The Density Deception: Never Trust Your Eyes Alone

The cardinal sin of cannabis buying is trusting your eyes. You’ve likely heard the industry myth that “if it looks big, it must be heavy.” We’re here to tell you that’s garbage. Density is the great deceiver in the world of flower, and it’s why a visual inspection will absolutely get you shorted.

Consider two extremes: a handful of fluffy, air-cured outdoor flower and a handful of rock-hard, high-density indoor indica nugs. The fluffy stuff, full of air pockets, can look like a legitimate quarter of an ounce—a monstrous, heaping pile—but actually weigh closer to an eighth. Conversely, those dense, crystalline nuggets can look minuscule, like a measly three grams, but clock in at the full seven. The volume-to-weight ratio is all over the map, making a visual check utterly useless. The only metric that matters is weight. If you’re buying based on volume, you’re playing a sucker’s game. Never buy based on volume; the only real measure of how many grams in a quarter is the scale.

Your Tool of Truth: Why Every Buyer Needs a Digital Scale

If you don’t own a scale, you are willingly outsourcing the integrity of your purchase to someone else—the easiest way to get shorted. The fix is cheap and mandatory: invest in a precise digital scale. You can find one with a $0.01$ gram precision for the cost of a single gram, making it the most immediate return on investment you’ll ever make in this industry. Trust us, it pays for itself on the first successful weight check.

Once you have your scale, mastery is simple:

  1. Placement: Place the container (bag, jar, tray) you will weigh your flower in onto the scale.
  2. Tare: Press the “Tare” or “Zero” button. This function subtracts the weight of the container, effectively zeroing out the scale so it only measures the flower. If you skip this, you’re just weighing the bag, you amateur.
  3. Weigh: Add the product. The final number is the only truth.

Beyond protecting your money, knowing the exact weight is a crucial layer of self-protection. Most jurisdictions have strict possession limits, and being a single gram over that limit because you trusted the seller is not an argument a judge will want to hear. Your scale is not just for preventing a short; it’s for staying compliant.

Quick Reality Check: Your Next Move

Let’s be direct. If you skimmed the last 3,000 words, here is your one, non-negotiable takeaway: A quarter is 7 grams. Period. There is no geographical exception, no “strain variance,” and no secret, industry-only conversion rate. Anyone telling you otherwise is either trying to confuse you or swindle you.

Your final warning: do not fall for the “it looks heavy” trap. That’s pure beginner talk. Forget visual estimation. The true sign of expertise—and maximum value—is simple: always calculate your actual price-per-gram on your next purchase. Divide the total price by 7 to see what you’re really paying. Demand the right amount and never leave value on the table again.
