What is Your Gross Hourly Rate, Anyway?

Before the government and your benefits provider raid your wallet, you need to know your gross hourly rate. That’s the nice, fake number before any deductions.

To figure this out, we assume a standard full-time work year.

  • 52 weeks in a year.
  • 40 hours in a work week.
  • That equals 2,080 working hours per year.

So, for an $85,000 salary, you just divide that number by 2,080.

$$\text{\$85,000} \div \text{2,080 hours} \approx \textbf{\$40.87 per hour}$$

Hot take: This number is a lie. A beautiful, tempting lie. It’s what the company pays for you, but it‚Äôs not what you get.

üëª The Payroll Ghost: Where Your Money Vanishes

You get paid \$40.87 for an hour of your life. But when you check your bank account, you‚Äôre only getting $25 or $30 per hour. Where did the rest go?

It‚Äôs simple, but annoying: Taxes and deductions. This money disappears right off the top before it even gets to your checking account.

Federal and State Taxes: The Biggest Chunk

This is the big one. Your gross pay gets hit with federal income tax first. We have a progressive tax system in the U.S. That just means you pay a higher tax rate on the dollars you earn at the top of your salary than the ones you earn at the bottom.

An $85,000 salary usually lands you in the 22% federal tax bracket (if you’re single, for example). But you don’t pay 22% on all of it. You only pay that rate on the part of your income over a certain amount. Confusing, right? That‚Äôs why using a calculator is easier than doing it by hand.

Then there are state and local taxes. If you live in a state like Texas or Florida, congrats! Your state tax is $0. If you live in California or New York, prepare for another chunk to vanish.

FICA Taxes: For Grandma and the Doctor

FICA stands for the Federal Insurance Contributions Act. Sounds super corporate, I know. But this is just your money for Social Security and Medicare.

  • Social Security: This is 6.2% of your income. It pays for retirement benefits.
  • Medicare: This is 1.45% of your income. It pays for health care for older people.

Together, that’s 7.65% that gets pulled out of every paycheck. It‚Äôs for a good reason, but it still shrinks that hourly rate.

Pre-Tax Deductions: The Good Kind of Vanishing Act

These are the things you pay for before the government calculates your taxes. And here’s the thing: They‚Äôre actually a good deal because they lower your taxable income.

Think of it like getting a discount on your taxes.

  • Health Insurance: Your monthly premium for medical, dental, or vision. Could be a few hundred bucks a month.
  • 401(k) or Retirement: The money you put toward your future.
  • HSA/FSA: Health savings or flexible spending account contributions.

If you put $500 a month toward all of this, that‚Äôs $6,000 less you pay taxes on for the whole year. Sweet, right? But it‚Äôs still money that‚Äôs not in your checking account now.

ü§ì The Simple Math: How to Turn $85K Into an Hourly Wage

Look, you don’t need a fancy calculator for this. You just need to know the magic number for full-time work.

Most full-time jobs assume you work 40 hours a week for 52 weeks a year. That gives you a nice, round number of 2,080 working hours per year.

The Foolproof Formula

To go from that huge annual salary down to a simple hourly rate, you just do one easy division problem:

$$\text{Annual Salary} \div \text{Working Hours per Year} = \text{Gross Hourly Wage}$$

So, for $85,000 a year, the math is:

$$85,000 \div 2,080 = \mathbf{\$40.87}$$

  • Your Gross Hourly Wage: $40.87 per hour

See? That’s all the industry experts do. It’s not rocket science.

Quick Reality Check: The ‘Rule of Thumb’

Want an even faster way to estimate? Forget the 2,080 number.

Take your salary (in thousands, so $85$ for $85,000) and divide it by 2.

$$85 \div 2 = 42.5$$

This gives you a super close estimate of $42.50 per hour. It’s not exact, but it’s perfect for quickly checking if a job posting is a good deal.

So, you’re making $40.87 an hour. Fantastic! You think you’re going to see all of that in your bank account, right?

Wrong. That $40.87 is your gross pay. It’s the money before the government and your employer take their cut.

Here’s the thing: Taxes are the biggest buzzkill in personal finance.

  • Federal Income Tax
  • State Income Tax (if you live in a state that has one)
  • Social Security
  • Medicare

Plus, you probably have other stuff coming out, like your health insurance and maybe your 401(k) retirement savings.

ü§Ø The Hidden Trap: When Salary Actually Means Less

This is my biggest hot take on the salary vs. hourly debate.

When you’re paid hourly ($40.87/hr), you’re often eligible for overtime. Work more than 40 hours in a week? You get paid “time and a half” (1.5x your rate). That’s $61.30 per hour!

But when you’re paid a salary (that $85,000), you’re usually what’s called an “exempt” employee.

The Salaried Scam

Here’s the scam: Your employer can make you work 50, 60, or even 70 hours a week, and you still get the exact same $85,000.

  • 40 hours/week: You make $40.87/hr. Good job.
  • 50 hours/week: You’re now making $32.69/hr. Yikes.
  • 60 hours/week: Your real rate just dropped to $27.18/hr. Ouch.

See how that big $85,000 number suddenly looks a little less shiny? You’re giving away hours for free.

The Point Is: If you’re considering a salaried job, always ask what the typical working hours are. If they say “around 50,” be real with yourself‚Äîyou’re accepting a much lower hourly rate.

These deductions aren’t taxes, but they still come out of your check. Some are optional, some are mandatory, and some are actually a good way to save money on taxes.

1. Retirement Savings (401k/IRA)

Why it‚Äôs awesome: Let’s say you put $500 a month into your 401(k). That means you only get taxed on the remaining $\$6,583$ of your monthly gross pay, not the full $\$7,083$. You save for retirement and you lower your tax bill. Winning!

2. Health Insurance Premiums

If you get health insurance through your job, the monthly cost of that insurance (the premium) is also usually a pre-tax deduction. You pay less in taxes, but that money is gone from your paycheck.

3. Other Stuff

You might also see deductions for:

  • Dental/Vision Insurance
  • Life Insurance
  • Flexible Spending Accounts (FSA) or Health Savings Accounts (HSA)

You need to check your pay stub for these every time, because a new 401(k) contribution or a different insurance plan can change your take-home pay right away.

üì∏ Mistake 1: Ignoring the Monster-Sized Images

You know those gorgeous, high-resolution photos you take? The ones that look stunning on your huge desktop screen?

Yeah, those are probably destroying your load time.

Big, uncompressed image files are the number one site speed killer. It’s like trying to mail an entire refrigerator when you just need to send a letter.

Every time a visitor clicks on your page, their browser has to download that entire refrigerator-sized file. If you have ten of those images? Forget about it.

Quick Fix: Make Your Pictures Skinny

You need to “compress” your images. That just means making the file size smaller without making the picture look terrible.

  • Don’t skip the compression step. Use a free tool like TinyPNG or an image plugin on your site. This simple step can shrink file sizes by 50% or more. Huge win!
  • Resize before you upload. If the photo is going into a small area on your blog, don’t upload the 5000-pixel-wide original. Resize it to something reasonable, like 1200 pixels wide, before you put it on your site.
  • Try a modern format like WebP. This is a newer, better way to save images that loads faster than old JPEGs. Your image optimization plugin can usually handle this switch for you automatically.

The point is, your browser has to work less, and your readers see your content faster. Win-win.

üò¥ Mistake 2: Letting Ads and Plugins Pile Up
