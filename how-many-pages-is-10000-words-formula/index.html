  • It’s a small change, but it makes a difference on a big document like a 10,000-word thesis or report.

  • Big quotes are often indented, which also adds empty space.

So, if your 10,000-word document is formatted nicely with lots of white space and easy-to-read lists, it’s going to be longer than the simple math suggests. That’s why my 10,000-word blog post might be 21 pages, but your friend’s research paper is 19.

üìê The Easy Formula for Your Page Count

You don’t need to guess anymore. Here’s the one simple formula you can use to figure out the page count for any word count, no matter how weird your font is.

Step 1: Find Your Words-Per-Page (WPP) Number

First, you have to do a quick test. This number is what tells you how many words fit on one page of your current setup.

  1. Open a new document in the format you need (single-spaced, 12pt Arial, etc.).
  2. Type or paste about 500 words of filler text. (Use a “lorem ipsum” generator if you don’t want to type.)
  3. Look at the word count for the text you pasted.
  4. Check how many full pages that text takes up.
  5. Divide the total words by the number of pages.

Example: You pasted 500 words, and it took up 2 full, double-spaced pages. 500 words / 2 pages = 250 WPP. That’s your magic number!

Step 2: Use the Word Count Formula

Once you have your WPP, the math is super simple.

$$\text{Total Pages} = \frac{\text{Total Word Count}}{\text{Words Per Page (WPP)}}$$

For our 10,000 words example, it breaks down like this:

  • If you got 500 WPP (single-spaced): $10,000 / 500 = 20 pages$
  • If you got 250 WPP (double-spaced): $10,000 / 250 = 40 pages$

That’s it. No more staring blankly at your screen. You have the tool to get a precise answer every single time.
