You can’t stop doing laundry‚Äîthat would be chaos. But you can do a few simple things to save your machine from an early retirement. Think of these as the three crucial factors that are either saving your machine or sending it straight to the appliance afterlife.

Here’s a sneaky one: using the right amount of water. If you always choose the “small load” setting but have a half-full drum, the machine is still struggling to move the clothes around efficiently.

More importantly, if you have hard water, those tiny mineral bits in the water are scraping away at your machine’s internals and hoses with every cycle. When it comes to washing machines, we basically have two types: the classic top-loader with the agitator, and the sleek front-loader with the complicated door. But which one is built to last longer?

Front-load washers are generally more water- and energy-efficient, and they get clothes cleaner with less wear and tear. That’s great! But the design is way more complex, and complexity means more stuff to break.

The average front-loader lifespan is about 11 to 14 years, which is now almost on par with top-loaders thanks to better engineering. But they have more common, specific issues:

  • HE vs. Standard: High-Efficiency (HE) washers‚Äîwhich are almost all front-loaders and some modern top-loaders‚Äîuse less water. So, while a top-loader with simple guts might run for 14 years with fewer repairs, a front-loader will save you money on utilities for 11 to 14 years but has a higher chance of needing a major repair, like bearings, that costs more than the machine is worth. Choose your poison!
