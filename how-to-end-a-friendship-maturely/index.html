This works best for friends you care about but just don’t click with anymore, or for friends you mostly see in a group setting. It’s like turning down the volume on the friendship until it’s barely a whisper.

  • Respond to texts slowly and vaguely. Don’t ignore them completely. But instead of an immediate, excited “OMG YES! Let’s do it!” try: “Oh, that sounds fun, I’ll check my calendar and let you know.” And then… don’t.
  • Keep your reasons short and simple. If they push for a hangout, don’t invent a detailed story about your sick aunt’s dog. Say you’re “super busy with work right now” or “trying to keep my social calendar light for a bit.” End of story.
  • Don’t share personal stuff anymore. Friendships thrive on shared secrets and details. When they ask about your life, keep your answers short and surface-level. You’re building a wall, brick by small brick.

  2. Make it brief. Say what you need to say, then let the silence land. Don’t feel the need to fill it with rambling apologies or justifications.
  3. Hold the line. They will probably try to argue, fix it, or make you feel guilty. Don’t get dragged back in. Just repeat your core message gently.

When you decide it’s time to end a friendship, you basically have two main options. And look, the decision depends on the friend, the history, and why you‚Äôre breaking up.

This requires actual courage. You sit them down (or hop on a quick call, honestly) and you tell them the truth. But remember: Honesty doesn’t mean you have to be mean.

How it works:

  • Pick the right time: Don’t do it over text or in a huge group setting. Find a quiet, private moment.
  • Use “I” statements: This is the most important part. Don’t say, “You always do this.” Say, “I need to step back from this friendship,” or “I feel like we’re not connecting anymore.” Keep the focus on your needs, not their flaws.
  • Be clear, but kind: You don’t have to list their sins. Just say something simple, like: “I really value our past, but honestly, I think we’re going in different directions now. I need to take a break from our friendship.”
  • Set the boundary: Let them know this is final. Don’t leave the door open for weekly coffee dates if you don’t mean it.

When to use this: Use this for close friends, long-term friendships, or when the friendship is ending because of a serious breach of trust or toxicity. They deserve the clarity, even if it hurts.
