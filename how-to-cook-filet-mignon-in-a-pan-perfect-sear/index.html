ü•© The Steak Prep: Don’t Mess This Up

Look, you bought the nice cut. It‚Äôs expensive. So don’t skip the crucial first step. If you want a perfectly cooked filet mignon in a pan, you have to start with a dry, room-temp steak.

Let It Warm Up (Seriously)

This might sound like a weird step, but trust me, it‚Äôs a non-negotiable part of cooking filet mignon.

Take your steak out of the fridge about 30‚Äì60 minutes before you plan to start cooking. I know, a full hour feels like forever, but just do it.

A cold steak hits a hot pan and instantly cools it down. This makes the cooking uneven. It‚Äôs how you end up with a gray, overcooked outside and a still-raw middle. Gross.

Letting it warm up means it cooks more evenly, which is the whole point of a fancy cut like this.

Pat It Dry Like a Newborn Baby

You want a good sear, right? That crispy, brown crust that makes your mouth water?

To get that amazing crust, you need to remove all the moisture from the surface of the meat. Water is the enemy of a good sear.

Grab some paper towels and pat down that filet mignon until it‚Äôs bone dry. It should feel almost sticky. If the steak is wet, the pan has to steam off the water before it can start browning the meat. And that‚Äôs how you get a sad, soggy steak instead of a killer crust.

Seasoning: Salt, Salt, Salt

This cut is tender and doesn‚Äôt need much. Just use kosher salt and freshly cracked black pepper.

Don’t be shy with the salt. You need more than you think, because a lot of it falls off in the pan. Season the steak aggressively on all sides, right before it goes into the pan.

üî• Time to Sear: The Hot Pan is Your Best Friend

This is the main event. Getting the pan hot enough is key. It’s like turning your home kitchen into a steakhouse.

Choose Your Pan and Your Fat

First, ditch the non-stick pan. You need something that holds heat like a champ, so grab a cast iron pan if you have one. If not, a heavy stainless steel pan will work.

Now, for the fat. You need something with a high smoke point so it doesn’t burn instantly. I prefer grapeseed or canola oil for the initial sear, because olive oil will just smoke up your kitchen.

Put the pan on high heat. You want it hot enough that a drop of water sizzles and evaporates immediately. But don’t let the oil start smoking heavily‚Äîthat means it’s burning.

Sear the Sides (No Flipping Yet!)

Once the pan is screaming hot, gently lay the filet mignon in the pan. Hear that sizzle? That‚Äôs the sound of magic happening.

Don’t touch it! Don’t look at it funny! Just leave it alone for 3‚Äì4 minutes. This is the time it takes to build that beautiful, dark crust. If you try to move it too early, it will stick.

After those minutes are up, flip the steak. Sear the other side for another 3‚Äì4 minutes. Now you’ve got a fantastic, deep-brown color on both sides.

üßà The Butter Baste: Flavor Overload

This is the part that takes your steak from “good” to “I need this every week.” It‚Äôs called basting, and it‚Äôs how the pros do it.

Add Butter, Garlic, and Herbs

Turn the heat down to medium-low. If you leave it on high, the butter will just burn.

Toss in a few tablespoons of unsalted butter, a couple of smashed cloves of garlic (no need to peel them), and a sprig or two of fresh thyme or rosemary.

As the butter melts, it should start foaming. Tip the pan so the butter pools on one side.

The Basting Technique

Grab a big spoon and start tilting the pan so the hot, melted butter mixture runs toward the bottom edge.

Use the spoon to scoop up the melted butter and pour it over the top of the steak, coating it completely. Do this continuously for about 1‚Äì2 minutes, flipping the steak halfway through and basting the other side.

The herb and garlic butter seeps into the crust, cooking the steak gently and adding unbelievable flavor. This also helps finish the cooking evenly.

Know When It’s Done

Here’s the honest truth: the only way to know if your filet mignon is cooked perfectly is to use a meat thermometer. Don’t guess. You paid too much for this steak to play Russian roulette with doneness.

  • Rare: $125^\circ F$
  • Medium-Rare: $130^\circ F$ (This is the ideal temp, trust me)
  • Medium: $135^\circ F$
  • Medium-Well: $145^\circ F$ (Please don’t go this high, you‚Äôll dry it out)

Pull the steak out of the pan about $5^\circ F$ before it reaches your target temperature.

You did it! The steak looks amazing. Now, you have to fight the urge to cut into it immediately.

The most important step in cooking any steak is the rest.

Take the filet mignon out of the pan and place it on a cutting board or a clean plate. Tent it loosely with foil‚Äîdon’t wrap it tightly.

Let it rest for at least 5 to 10 minutes. This gives the juices time to redistribute throughout the meat. If you cut it too soon, all those beautiful, savory juices will just run out onto the board, and you’ll be left with a dry steak. Sad!

This is huge. If your steak is wet, that moisture will turn into steam in the hot pan. Steam stops your meat from browning. That means no beautiful, crunchy crust. You just get a sad, soft, gray surface.

The Salt Bomb: Seasoning Like You Mean It

A tiny sprinkle of salt just isn’t going to cut it here. You need to use a coarse salt, like kosher salt, and you need to use a lot of it. Think of it like a light coat of sand. This helps pull even more moisture out and builds an amazing layer of flavor.

Don’t be shy with the pepper either. Season both sides right before you‚Äôre about to cook. It helps create the initial crust and flavor. We’re not using fancy rubs, just a generous amount of salt and pepper.

Put your cast iron skillet on high heat and let it get screaming hot. I mean, hot enough that a drop of water would evaporate instantly. Add a high-smoke-point oil, like canola or grapeseed, and let that heat up too. You need just enough to lightly coat the bottom‚Äîdon’t drown the steak.
