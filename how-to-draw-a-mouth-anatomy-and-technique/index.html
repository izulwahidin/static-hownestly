  • To draw a relaxed expression: The central line is very close to straight, maybe a tiny bit of an ‘S’ curve. The corners are mostly horizontal.

The lesson here is to look at the muscles around the mouth, not just the lips themselves. That little mound of muscle above the top lip and the fleshy part under the bottom lip? Those change shape and cast shadows, too.

Forget everything you think you know about lips for a second. The biggest mistake people make is not seeing the mouth as part of a sphere or a cylinder that holds the teeth.

  • The Philtrum: This is that little groove that runs from the bottom of your nose down to the center of your upper lip. It acts like a key landmark, lining up the whole center of the mouth. Hot tip: The two little ridges that make up the philtrum push the middle of the upper lip out.
  • The Cupid’s Bow: The fancy name for the little double-curve in the center of the upper lip. Everyone has one, and playing with this shape changes the whole look of the mouth. Want a soft, full lip? Make it a gentle curve. Want something sharper? Make a clear “M” shape.
  • The Commissures (Mouth Corners): These are where the upper and lower lips meet and connect. The key here is that they pinch inward, creating a little bump of shadow and skin. They don’t just stop in a sharp point. They’re more like little knots where all the facial muscles tie together.

The Point Is: The mouth has depth. It sticks out from the face! The upper lip often angles down and is in shadow, and the lower lip angles up and catches the light. This light-and-shadow play is what makes it look 3D.

Look, here‚Äôs the biggest piece of anatomy you need: The mouth isn’t flat like a sticker on a tennis ball. It wraps around the skull, which is basically a curved surface.

When someone smiles, the corners of their mouth pull back. They don’t just stay put. They actually disappear slightly around the side of that imaginary cylinder shape.

  • Use shading to show the wrap. As the lip curves away toward the cheek, the light hits it less directly, so it gets darker. This little bit of darker shading at the corners is crucial for showing depth.
  • The actual lips stop a little before the corners. The last bit of the opening is just skin curving back into the face. This is what makes it look like it’s actually set into the skin.

Another detail people forget? The area right between your nose and your top lip. It’s called the philtrum, and it’s that little vertical groove.
