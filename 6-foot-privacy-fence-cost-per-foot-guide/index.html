Beyond 6-Feet: Smart Alternatives and Nuanced Privacy Fence Hacks

A great fence isn’t just about the cost per foot; it’s about solving your privacy problem efficiently. Before you sign the contract, consider these advanced strategies that can reduce your total outlay or provide better performance.

The ‘Good Neighbor’ Problem: Shadowbox vs. Board-on-Board

When you buy a fence, one side looks great (the smooth side) and one side looks like the messy backside where the posts and rails are. Your neighbor is going to complain if they get stuck with the messy side.

That’s why the Shadowbox style is super common. It puts the boards on alternating sides of the rail, so both sides look “good” and you still get privacy. However, it uses more material than the standard style and costs a little more per foot.

Hot tip: Check your local rules. Some towns actually require you to put the “good side” (the finished side) facing out toward the street or your neighbor.

When a 6-Foot Privacy Fence Isn’t the Right Choice (And What To Do Instead)

Total, solid privacy is great, but it has downsides. You need to know them.

In high wind areas, a solid 6-foot fence acts like a huge sail. It needs much stronger posts set deeper‚Äîwhich adds significant cost. If you live somewhere windy, you might want to switch to a semi-private design (like shadowbox) or even drop the height to 5 feet.

Also, a solid wall fence can stop air and light from moving, which can hurt your landscaping. If you have a beautiful garden, consider adding a lattice topper to the top of the fence. This keeps the privacy low, but lets air and light through the top.
