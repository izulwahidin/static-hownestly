Step 1: Identify Your Fob and the Battery

Before you go hunting for a tiny Phillips-head screwdriver (spoiler: you don’t need one), you need to correctly identify your fob’s type and its power source. Subaru uses a couple of different smart key designs, but for the vast majority of models from roughly 2014 onward (Outback, Forester, Crosstrek, Impreza, etc.), the battery is the ubiquitous, inexpensive CR2032 3-volt lithium coin cell. This is the single most common battery in the key fob universe‚Äîdon’t let anyone sell you a “Subaru-specific” battery for $10.

  • Battery Specification: For most modern Subaru key fobs, the required battery is the CR2032. Buy a name-brand one; they genuinely last longer.
  • Locate the Seam: Your fob has a subtle, hair-thin seam running along the edge. This is your target. You are looking for a small, often hidden release button or sliding switch that allows the metal emergency or valet key to be pulled out.
  • The Valet Key’s Dual Purpose: That metal key is not just for getting into the door when the battery dies; it’s also your primary tool for separating the casing.

Step 2: Safely Crack the Casing with the Valet Key

This is the most crucial step, as a careless attempt will result in a permanently scarred plastic key fob, a testament to your impatience.

  • Remove the Valet Key: First, press the small release button or switch and pull the metal emergency key out. Keep it handy.
  • Find the Pry Point: On most Subaru fobs (like the modern smart key), once the valet key is out, you’ll see a small, rectangular notch inside the empty key slot. This is the manufacturer-designated pry point. It’s designed specifically for the thickness of the metal key you just removed.
  • Twist to Separate: Insert the tip of the metal valet key into that notch. Don’t try to lever it straight up; instead, apply gentle pressure and twist the key 90 degrees as if you were turning a flat-head screw. This twisting motion generates an even, gentle force that cleanly separates the two halves of the plastic casing with a satisfying click.

Step 3: The Swift Swap and Casing Reassembly

Once the casing is separated, you’ll see the circuit board and the coin cell battery held in place by a small metal clip or plastic frame.

  • New Battery Insertion: Slide the fresh CR2032 under the clips, ensuring the positive (+) side is facing up (it usually has the branding/text on it).   • Reassembly Check: Snap the two halves of the plastic casing back together‚Äîthey should mate seamlessly without any gap. Then, slide the metal valet key back into its slot until it locks. Test the key fob immediately by locking/unlocking your vehicle from a few feet away.

CR2032 vs. CR1632: Why Battery Size Is Your First Check

Many older, beloved Subaru models‚Äîlike the Forester (2014-2018) or the Impreza (2014-2016) that use the physical flip-key‚Äîwere originally engineered for the CR1632 battery. The Key-Slot Trick: Using the Valet Key to Pry Open the Casing

Contrary to what the YouTube “hackers” might show you, you don’t need a flat-head screwdriver that will inevitably scratch your plastic. You already have the perfect tool built right into your fob: the valet key.

First, identify the small release button or slider‚Äîusually on the back or side of the fob‚Äîand slide it to remove the physical, metal ‘valet’ key. Now you have two things: the key itself and a small, rectangular hole (the key-slot) on the fob casing.

  • Insert the tip of your tool or the flat end of the valet key into the key-slot, just enough to catch the inner seam.
  • Instead of pulling or prying straight out, twist the tool gently. This twisting action applies pressure evenly to the inner clips, forcing the casing to pop open precisely where it was designed to.
  • Do not insert your tool or key into the long, thin edge where the front and back pieces meet. If you feel resistance that requires actual effort, you are prying against a load-bearing plastic clip and are about to break it. You should only feel a gentle “pop” as the case separates.

The goal is precision, not muscle. If your Subaru fob looks like it was attacked by a rabid badger after your battery change, you did it wrong. Stick to the key-slot, and your fob will maintain its structural integrity‚Äîand resale value.

Limitations: When to Avoid DIY and Visit the Dealer

The difference between a successful DIY fix and an expensive mistake is knowing when to stop. There are hard limits to what a simple battery replacement can fix, and pushing past them can sometimes make the problem worse.

DIY is also a non-starter if the key fob casing is clearly compromised.
