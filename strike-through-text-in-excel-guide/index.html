The Old School Way: ‘Format Cells’ (And Why It’s Usually Too Slow)

Before we dive into the good stuff, let’s acknowledge the method everyone probably stumbles upon first. It works, sure, but it’s the dial-up modem of Excel formatting. We’ll show you how, and then promptly show you why you’ll want to move on.

Right-Click Ritual: The ‘Format Cells’ Dialog Step-by-Step

Okay, so you’ve got a cell in Excel. Maybe it has a task you just finished, or a budget item you wisely decided against. And you want to put a line through it. Your first instinct, like a tiny Excel detective, is probably to right-click. Go on, try it!

Once you right-click that cell, a whole menu pops up. It’s a lot, right? But don’t panic. You’re looking for an option usually near the bottom that says “Format Cells…” Click that, and boom, a new window appears. This is your command center for making cells look pretty (or, in this case, less pretty with a line through them).

Now, inside this new window, you’ll see a bunch of tabs across the top: Number, Alignment, Font, Border, Fill, Protection. For our strikethrough mission, you need the ‘Font’ tab. See it there? Click it.

And just like magic (or, you know, good software design), right in the middle section under “Effects,” you’ll find a little box labeled “Strikethrough.” Give that bad boy a checkmark. Hit “OK” at the bottom, and voilà! Your text now has a line through it. Mission accomplished, technically.

But here’s the thing: Is this acceptable? Absolutely, if you’re doing this once a year for a single cell. Maybe your grandma wants to learn Excel, and this is her first tiny victory. But for anything more, it’s like trying to fill a swimming pool with a teaspoon. There are better ways. Much better.

Your New Best Friend: The Instant Strikethrough Shortcut (Ctrl+5)

Forget digging through menus. The keyboard shortcut is your express lane to striking through text in Excel. This is where real efficiency begins.

The Universal Power of Ctrl+5 (And Its Mac Counterpart)

Okay, look. Sometimes, you just need to say, “Nope, not happening.” Or “This is old news.” Or maybe, “Budget item officially cancelled.” And that’s exactly what strikethrough is for. It’s like drawing a line through something in real life, but way neater.

For all you Windows warriors out there, your magic spell is Ctrl+5. Yep, that’s it. Hold down the ‘Ctrl’ key, then hit the number ‘5’ at the top of your keyboard (not the number pad, usually). Poof! Text crossed out. It’s like a light switch for your data. You hit it once to turn on the strikethrough, and you hit it again to turn it off. Simple, right? No more wrestling with font settings or trying to remember which tab has that tiny little “abc” icon.

Now, for our friends rocking a Mac, you’re not left out. Apple likes to do things a little differently, of course. Your secret handshake is Cmd+Shift+X. Hold down ‘Command’ (that’s the one with the squiggly pretzel symbol), ‘Shift’, and then hit the ‘X’ key. It does the exact same thing. And yes, it’s also a toggle switch. Hit it once, it’s crossed out. Hit it again, it’s back to normal. See? No more excuses for messy spreadsheets. Just clean, quick edits. You’re welcome.

Precision Strikes: Whole Cells vs. Specific Text Within a Cell

So, you know the shortcut. Awesome. But here’s the thing: sometimes you need to zap an entire cell, and other times, it’s just a tiny bit of text within it. Good news: your trusty shortcut works for both, you just gotta know the trick.

Wanna cross out everything in a cell? Easy peasy. Click on the cell you want to punish. Just select the whole thing. Don’t double-click, just a single click. Then, hit your shortcut (that’s Ctrl+5 for Windows, or Cmd+Shift+X for Mac). Boom! The entire cell’s content gets that satisfying line through it. Think of it when you’re scrapping an entire task on a to-do list, or maybe a whole budget line item that’s been officially rejected.

But what if only part of your text is getting the axe? Like, maybe you wrote “Send email to Bob and Sally” but you already sent it to Sally. You don’t need to delete everything. Nope. Just double-click on the cell to go into edit mode. Then, highlight only the text you want to strike through. So, in our example, you’d highlight “and Sally.” Now, hit your shortcut again. See? Only “and Sally” gets the line. It’s perfect for minor corrections or marking specific parts as done without trashing the whole message. Pretty slick, huh?

Customizing Your Ribbon or QAT for Instant Access

Look, we’ve all been there. You’re trying to mark a line item in your spreadsheet as “nope, not happening,” and suddenly you’re clicking through a labyrinth of menus. It’s like a scavenger hunt, but the prize is just… strikethrough. Not exactly a pot of gold, is it?

But what if you could just click one button? Imagine the sheer power! That’s where the Quick Access Toolbar (QAT) comes in. Think of the QAT as your personal VIP section for the commands you use most often. It’s that tiny row of icons usually chilling at the very top left of your Excel window, just above the main menu (which Microsoft calls the “Ribbon,” by the way).

Adding strikethrough to your QAT is super easy, almost laughably so. And once you do it, you’ll wonder how you ever lived without it.

Here’s the lowdown, step-by-step:

  1. Spot the tiny arrow: Head up to your QAT. See that little down arrow on the far right of those icons? Click it. This opens a small menu.
  2. More Commands, please: From that menu, choose “More Commands…” This opens up a whole new window, giving you the keys to your Excel kingdom.
  3. Find your treasure: In the left column of this new window, where it says “Choose commands from:”, click the dropdown and select “All Commands.” Now, buckle up, because this list is long.
  4. Scroll and conquer: Scroll through the alphabetized list until you find “Strikethrough.” (It’s usually pretty far down, past “Sort” and before “Subtotal.”)
  5. Add it to the party: Select “Strikethrough” and then click the “Add >>” button in the middle. Boom! It jumps over to your custom list on the right.
  6. Make it official: Click “OK” at the bottom right.

And just like that, you’ve got a shiny new strikethrough button ready for action. No more hunting, no more guessing. Just glorious, instant crossing out. You’re basically a productivity ninja now.

Setting Up Rules for Task Completion or Error Flagging

Alright, let’s start with the basics – making Excel strikethrough text when a specific word pops up, like “Done” or “Error.” This is super handy for any kind of list, whether it’s tracking chores, project steps, or your ever-growing pile of Netflix shows to watch.

Think of it like this: you’re telling Excel, “Hey, if this cell says this, then make it look that way.” In our case, “that way” means a cool strikethrough. Simple, right?

Here’s how you actually do it, step-by-step:

  1. Pick your target: First, select the cells you want this rule to apply to. Maybe it’s your whole to-do list, or just a specific column. Let’s say it’s column A, where your tasks are listed.
  2. Find the magic button: Head over to the Home tab in Excel. You’ll see a section called “Styles.” Inside that, click on Conditional Formatting. It looks kinda fancy, but trust me, it’s your new best friend.
  3. Start a new adventure: From the dropdown menu, choose New Rule… This opens up a little box with a bunch of options. Don’t panic!
  4. Choose your weapon: Select the option that says “Format only cells that contain.” This is where we tell Excel to look for specific words.
  5. Tell it what to look for: In the next section, you’ll see a few dropdowns.
    • For the first one, keep it as “Cell Value.”
    • The second one, change it to “equal to.”
    • In the box next to that, type the word you want to trigger the strikethrough. Let’s go with “Completed” (without the quotes, obviously). You can also do “Error” or “Done” or “Finished” – whatever works for your system.
  6. The big reveal (the strikethrough!): Now, click the Format… button. This opens up another box. See the “Font” tab? Click on that. You’ll spot a checkbox for Strikethrough. Check it! You can also change the font color if you want, maybe to a light gray to really make it look “done.”
  7. Seal the deal: Click OK on the Format Cells box, then OK again on the New Formatting Rule box.

And just like that, you’re done! Now, if you type “Completed” into any cell in your selected range, Excel will automatically cross out the text. No more manual clicking! It’s super satisfying, and way less effort. Frustrating spreadsheets, be gone!

Beyond Simple Checks: Formulas for Complex Conditions

Okay, so the “contains a word” trick is cool, but what if you need Excel to be even smarter? What if you want an entire row to get crossed out just because a date in another cell passed? Or maybe if a project manager marked something “Canceled” in a different column?

This is where the real brainpower of Conditional Formatting kicks in: using a formula to determine which cells to format. This is the advanced move, the power play, the moment you realize Excel isn’t just a spreadsheet, it’s a co-worker who actually listens.

Here’s the deal: instead of just checking if a cell itself has a specific value, you write a tiny bit of code (a formula!) that asks Excel a question. If the answer is “yes,” then BAM! Strikethrough.

Let’s dive into a couple of awesome examples:

Example 1: Striking Through a Row if a Date is Passed

Imagine you have a project list. Column A has the task, Column B has the deadline. You want the whole task row to get crossed out once the deadline in Column B passes.

  1. Select your range: Highlight all the rows and columns you want this rule to apply to. If your list is in A2:C100, select that entire block.
  2. New Rule, New Brain: Go back to Conditional Formatting > New Rule…
  3. The Formula Option: This time, choose “Use a formula to determine which cells to format.” This is where things get interesting.
  4. Write the magic formula: Click in the formula box and type: =B2<TODAY()
    • What does this mean? B2 is the first cell in your selected range that contains a deadline. The < means “is less than.” And TODAY() is an Excel function that just spits out today’s date. So, this formula asks, “Is the date in B2 earlier than today?”
    • Crucial Tip: Notice the B2? If your dates are in column C, it would be C2. Make sure you use the first cell of the column you’re checking against, without any dollar signs, unless you only want that specific cell to be checked. For an entire row to be formatted based on column B, you’d actually use =$B2<TODAY(). The dollar sign $B locks the column, but 2 allows the row number to change as Excel checks each row. Super important!
  5. Format it! Click Format…, go to the Font tab, and select Strikethrough. Click OK, then OK again.

Now, as soon as a date in Column B becomes a past date, the entire row (A, B, and C) will get a strikethrough. Pretty slick, right?

Example 2: Striking Through if Another Cell Says “Canceled”

Maybe you have a status column (let’s say Column C). If someone types “Canceled” in Column C, you want the whole row to be crossed out.

  1. Select your range: Again, highlight all the cells you want to be affected (e.g., A2:D100).
  2. New Rule > Use a formula… (you know the drill!).
  3. The formula: Type: =$C2="Canceled"
    • What’s happening? We’re asking, “Does the cell in Column C of this row (starting with C2) say ‘Canceled’?” The $ before C locks it to column C, so even if the rule applies to columns A, B, and D, they’ll all look at what’s happening in column C for their formatting cue.
  4. Strikethrough and done! Format it with strikethrough, hit OK.

These formula-based rules are the real game-changers. They give you incredible flexibility to automate your formatting based on almost any condition you can think up. It feels like you’ve unlocked a secret Excel level, and frankly, you have.

Quick Fixes: Shortcuts and Buttons to Erase Strikethrough

So, you’ve got some text with a line through it, and you’re ready for it to vanish. No worries, it’s usually super easy. Think of it like hitting rewind on your favorite show.

First up, remember that keyboard shortcut you used to draw a line through text in the first place? Well, guess what? It’s like a magic eraser! Just highlight the text you want to fix, then hit Ctrl+5 again (or Cmd+Shift+X if you’re rocking a Mac). And poof! The strikethrough disappears. It’s a toggle, like a light switch for your words. Pretty neat, right?

But maybe you’re more of a mouse-clicker. If you added the strikethrough button to your Quick Access Toolbar (that little row of buttons at the very top, usually near the save icon), just select your text and click that button again. Boom. Gone. It’s designed to be simple.

Now, for the nuclear option: the “Clear Formats” button. This one isn’t playing around. It wipes out everything – bold, italics, colors, even the fancy fonts you picked. So, use this one with caution, like deciding to cut all your hair off. It gets rid of the strikethrough, sure, but also everything else. Sometimes you just need a trim, not a buzz cut, know what I mean?

Managing Your Rules: Clearing Conditional Strikethrough

Okay, now for the trickier stuff: when your computer is being a little too helpful. Sometimes, that strikethrough isn’t just a simple button click. It’s living by rules.

This is called “conditional formatting,” which basically means “if this, then that.” Like, “if a task is done, draw a line through it.” Smart, but sometimes those rules hang around like a bad smell or that one piece of glitter that never leaves.

To kick those stubborn rules to the curb, head over to the “Home” tab in your spreadsheet program. Look for “Conditional Formatting,” then choose “Manage Rules.” This is where you play detective. You’ll see a list of all the rules currently making changes to your sheet. You can pick the specific rule that’s putting lines through your text and hit “Delete Rule.” Easy peasy, just like deleting an old text message.

Or, if you just want to nuke all the rules in a certain area, select those cells that are misbehaving. Go back to “Conditional Formatting,” but this time pick “Clear Rules.” You can clear from just your selection or the whole entire sheet. It’s like resetting your phone – sometimes you just gotta start fresh. And always double-check your work afterward. Make sure your sheet looks exactly how you want it, not like a digital Jackson Pollock painting!
