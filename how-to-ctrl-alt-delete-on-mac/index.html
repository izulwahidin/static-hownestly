Trying to hit $1500$ words just for the sake of it leads to something I call Fluff Writing. Know what I mean?

  • You repeat the same point three different ways.
  • You add a whole section that nobody needs.
  • You use ten examples when two would have been fine.

The second a reader sees a giant wall of text, or realizes you’re just stalling, they hit the back button. That’s a bad signal for Google, because it tells them: “This page didn’t actually help, and I wasted my time.” It doesn’t matter if you used your keyword five times; if people leave right away, you won’t rank.

Quick reality check: Google is smart now. It doesn’t just count words. It looks at Dwell Time (how long people stay) and Click-Through Rate (if people click your link). If people click, skim the intro, and run away, that $1500$ word count did nothing but hurt you.

üîé How to Find the Actual Perfect Blog Post Length

So, if $1500$ is wrong, what’s right? The best blog post length is always determined by the search result page itself. You need to become a private investigator for your keyword.

  • Scan the titles and the structure. Are they all super long guides? Are they short lists? Are they definitions?
  • Open the top 3-5 results. Don’t read every word (who has time for that?). Just scroll and look at how much content is there. Are they $800$ words or $2,500$?

Here’s the takeaway: Google is literally telling you what it thinks the best answer looks like. If every single result for your keyword is a short $600$-word definition, guess what? You don’t need $1500$ words. If they’re all massive guides, you need to write a massive guide, too.

2. Answer the “All-in-One” Question

The goal is to write a post that leaves no stone unturned for the person who Googled that topic. Think of it like this: What follow-up questions will they have?

  • If they search “how to make sourdough starter,” they’re going to wonder about things like “what if it doesn’t bubble?” or “how long does it take?”
  • Your job is to answer the main question and all those little sub-questions in one place.

If you can answer everything perfectly in $900$ words, stop there! If it takes $2,000$ to cover all the angles and make yours the definitive guide, then write $2,000$ words.

Your length is based on the topic’s complexity, not a random target. A post on “What is an avocado?” is naturally shorter than a post on “The Ultimate Guide to Growing Avocado Trees in a Cold Climate.” Simple as that.

Whatever the average length is for the top 5 competitors, you want to aim for that length‚Äîbut with better content. Don’t just make it longer, make it deeper.

  • Or, if the competition is weak, write a post that’s slightly longer, but only because you added a fantastic new section they completely missed.

Remember: Your post needs to be $10 \times$ better than the competition. Not $10 \times$ longer. Make sense? You need to give readers an “Aha!” moment the other posts didn’t.
