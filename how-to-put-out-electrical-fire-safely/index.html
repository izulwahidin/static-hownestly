The Smothering Strategy

The goal is to seal off the fire from the outside world, like putting a lid on a container. But you can’t just throw any old thing on it. You need something thick that won’t melt or catch fire itself. Think of it as a heavy, fire-proof blanket.

  • Go for the heavy stuff: Find a thick blanket, a heavy coat, or a rug. You want something made of natural, tightly woven material like wool or cotton. Wool is the best because it naturally resists fire.
  • Avoid the cheap stuff: Absolutely do not use synthetic fabrics, like nylon or polyester. Know what happens? They melt into a sticky, flaming goo that just makes the fire worse. Yuck.
  • Drape, don’t drop: You need to approach the fire carefully, keeping your body low, and gently drape the material over the fire. You want to cover the whole thing in one smooth move. Don’t just chuck it from across the room.
  • Seal the edges: Make sure the material is flat on the ground all the way around the fire’s base. You are creating a seal. If you see smoke escaping, it means air is getting in, and you’re just giving it fresh oxygen.

Once the fire is covered, leave it alone! Don’t peek. It needs time to suffocate completely. If you lift the blanket too early, you’re just giving the fire a breath of fresh air, and it’ll pop right back up. You can practically hear it laughing at you.

Get the Air Moving (Safely)

Your first priority has to be getting yourself out and making sure your escape route is clear. Don’t worry about the smoke until the fire is completely and totally out and the area is safe to be near.

Once the danger of the fire is gone, you need to ventilate the area immediately.

  • Open the windows and doors (that aren’t blocked by the smoke, obviously). You want to create a path for the smoke to escape outside.
  • Get fresh air in there to push the toxic smoke out.

But seriously, if you start feeling dizzy or sick from the smoke, get out. Don’t be a hero. You can replace a burnt-up laptop, but you can’t replace your lungs.

You know that little spot behind your entertainment center or under your office desk? The one that looks like a spaghetti monster made of wires? It’s chaos. And that chaos is a serious fire hazard waiting to happen.

High-draw means anything that creates heat or changes the temperature of something else. We’re talking space heaters, toasters, portable AC units, coffee makers, and hair dryers.
