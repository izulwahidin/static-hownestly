üé§ How to Handle Two Authors in Your Speech

When you’re citing a source with two authors, you basically treat them like a package deal. You need to say both of their last names. Skipping one is a big no-no; it’s like only crediting half the band for a hit song.

Say Both Last Names, Every Time

This is the golden rule for two authors. You just say “and” between their last names. Easy.

  • According to researchers Smith and Jones…
  • The 2023 study by Garcia and Lee found that…

But here’s the thing: You still need to give the source some muscle by mentioning where they published it or why they’re smart. You can’t just drop names and run.

  • Example: “In a 2022 article from the Journal of Psychology, Smith and Jones reported that social media use declined by 10%.”

See? You gave them the Author + Source/Credential + Date. That’s the perfect mix of short and credible.

üë©‚Äçüî¨ When It‚Äôs Three or More Authors (The Et al. Rule)

This is where it gets really simple. If you have a source written by three or more authors, you only say the last name of the very first author you see listed.

Why? Because you don’t want to burn up five minutes of your speech listing off names no one will remember. This is where you use the famous Latin shortcut: et al.

You Say “And Colleagues” Instead of “Et al.”

In writing, you’d use “et al.” (it just means “and others”). But you don’t say Latin words out loud in a speech‚Äîthat’s too stuffy.

You replace “et al.” with one of these casual phrases:

  • …and colleagues (This is the most common and professional.)

  • …and the research team

  • …and associates (A bit corporate, but totally fine.)

  • Example 1 (Starting the sentence): “A 2024 study by Miller and colleagues in the journal Nature suggests climate change models need to be updated.”

  • Example 2 (Ending the sentence): “The risk for this type of policy failure, according to a recent report from Patel and colleagues at the Brookings Institution, is now at an all-time high.”

Quick Reality Check: You only have to give the full, detailed citation the first time you use that source. After that, you can shorten it dramatically.

  • First time: “A 2024 study by Miller and colleagues in the journal Nature…”
  • Second time (later in the speech): “The same Miller study also pointed out…” or “As Miller‚Äôs team previously mentioned…”

üìö The Golden Rules of Oral Citation

Look, you’re not going for an A+ on a term paper. You’re trying to be persuasive. And the best way to do that is to make your evidence sound super credible without putting your audience to sleep.

  • Keep it Short: Don’t read the whole citation like it’s a legal document. Credibility first, word count second.
  • Mix it Up: Don’t use “According to…” every time. Try these instead:
    • “As noted by…”
    • “A recent report from…”
    • “The experts at [Organization] found that…”
    • “In his 2023 book, The Science of Winning, author John Clark argues…”
  • Give the Expert Tag: Always explain why they are worth listening to. Don’t just say “Sarah Chen.” Say, “Sarah Chen, a lead economist at Harvard…” This makes your evidence much heavier.

The point is this: Your audience can’t hit pause and Google your footnotes. You have to give them the key info in the moment. So be clear, be brief, and use “and colleagues” to save yourself from turning your speech into a roll call.

‚úÖ The “Good” Ways: Making Multiple Authors Sound Smooth

The secret to citing multiple authors orally is to group them by idea, not by name. You want to make it sound like all these experts agree with your point. And here’s the best part: you only need a few simple phrases.

1. The “Consensus” Method: Experts Agree…

This method is super powerful for persuasive speeches. It uses your multiple sources to show that there’s a big, wide agreement on your point. It makes your argument look strong because it’s not just one person saying it.

Here’s the structure you use:

  1. Introduce the big idea: Start with what all the sources are agreeing on.
  2. Use a strong grouping phrase: Say something like, “This is widely supported by research from multiple experts.”
  3. List the authors and dates: Give the names and dates smoothly afterward.

Example: “The idea that screen time messes up sleep cycles is almost undeniable at this point. This is a finding consistently reported by researchers like Dr. Chen in 2022, as well as the 2023 study from the Sleep Foundation, and the work of neuroscientist, Mark Davies.”

See how you put the names and dates at the end? You focus on the point first. You make the names a quick footnote, not the main event.

2. The “Building-Blocks” Method: Starting with…

Use this when you have sources that build on each other. One source laid the foundation, and the next source took the idea further. This shows your audience that you‚Äôve done a real deep dive into the research.

Here’s the structure you use:

  1. Acknowledge the first source: Use a phrase like, “Building on the foundational work of…”
  2. Connect to the next source: Use a clear transition like, “This concept was later expanded by…”

Example: “Building on the foundational work of economist Laura King in 2018, which first highlighted the drop in local spending, this concept was later expanded by a 2023 report from the City Planning Office, which found the overall impact was three times worse than King predicted.”

This makes you sound smart because you’re showing the history of the research. You’re telling a story, not just listing names. It’s like citing the first iPhone and then the current one‚Äîyou show the progression.

3. The “Team-Up” Method: In a Joint Effort…

Sometimes you just have a couple of sources that are simply in the same boat, both making the same claim. This is the simplest fix for those tricky spots where you need two authors for the same sentence.

Here’s the structure you use:

  1. Cite them together: Use a simple phrase that combines the two.
  2. Give the information: Follow it with the fact or statistic.

Example: “Both Dr. Elena Rodriguez and psychologist Ben Carter confirmed in separate studies last year that the use of a weighted blanket reduces anxiety by about 25 percent. This confirms our point perfectly.”

You make the two authors a single unit: Both $A$ and $B$. This avoids the awkward pause between names. Just be direct, and get the information out there.

üòÇ Seriously, Who Has Time for a Full Bibliography During a Speech?

Ever been in a persuasive speech and the speaker tries to read out a citation that sounds like a legal document? It’s awkward, right? You lose the audience the second you start listing off full names, journal titles, and a publication date from five years ago. Frustrating, right?

But here’s the deal: you have to cite your sources. Otherwise, it’s just your opinion, and that’s not very persuasive. When you’re using information from multiple people‚Äîlike a big study or an article with three authors‚Äîyou need a way to do it without sounding like a corporate robot.

So, how do you handle citing multiple authors orally in a persuasive speech? You just need to give the necessary credit without losing your momentum. It’s about being credible, not complicated. You’re going for expert friend, not boring professor.

üé§ Make the Authors a “Team” (and Skip the Credentials)

The biggest mistake people make is reading out every single author’s name. Look, if your source has five authors, nobody cares about all five of them. They just need to know it came from a credible place.

üåü Go with the First Name and the Magic Phrase

When you have a source by two or three authors, you should definitely try to mention all their last names. That shows you did your homework. But don’t say, “According to Dr. Elizabeth Hayes and Dr. Marcus Chen…” Keep it moving!

Here’s the quick-and-dirty way:

  • According to Hayes and Chen in their 2023 study…
  • A report by Smith, Jones, and Garcia from last year revealed that…
  • The authors of the Journal of Science article, Johnson and Lee, claim…

See how that works? You give them the names, you give them the date (because newer info is always better), and you’re done. You use the names, not the degrees they have.

üìù What If There Are a Ton of Authors?

Sometimes you get a source that’s like a party‚Äîway too many people involved. We’re talking four or more authors. Listing all those names will totally sink your speech. You don’t want to use up half your speaking time on a citation.

Here’s a secret for citing multiple authors: You only need to mention the first author’s last name.

  • Say: “A 2024 Harvard study, led by Professor Kim, found that…”
  • Or: “The research team, headed by Dr. Almas, showed…”

In written stuff, they use “et al.” (a fancy way to say “and others”). In a speech, just say “and colleagues” or “and their team.” That’s way more natural and less stuffy.

  • “New research from the University of London, by Peterson and colleagues in 2022, proves…”

You’re giving credit to the lead person and acknowledging it was a team effort. Done.

üéØ Focus on the Source’s VIBE, Not the Title

You’re giving a persuasive speech. That means your goal is to make your argument strong and believable. A source from The New York Times sounds way more credible than an article called “The Daily Ramblings of a Guy on the Internet.”

üì∞ The Holy Trinity of Oral Citing

When you cite a source, especially one with multiple authors, you need three things‚Äîand they don’t include the full title.

  1. Author (or the Lead Author/Team): “Peterson and his team” or “Hayes and Chen.”
  2. Date (the newer the better): “A 2023 study” or “published last year.”
  3. Source Credibility (the publisher): “in the journal Nature“ or “from a CNN poll.”

Bad Citation: “According to a study called ‘The Impact of Social Media on Teenagers’ published on February 14th, 2022…” üò¥ Good Citation: “According to a 2022 report from the Pew Research Center, the team found…”

The Pew Research Center part is the gold right there! That name has weight, and your audience knows it’s a solid source without you having to drone on.

Here’s the deal: Oral citation‚Äîthat just means saying where your info came from‚Äîis not the same as writing a paper. In a paper, you can bury it in a footnote. In a speech, it has to sound smooth, not like a corporate press release.

Why Your Awkward Citations Are Tanking Your Talk

Let’s start with a quick reality check. Why do we even cite things in a speech?

It’s not just to make your professor happy. It’s about something called credibility transfer. That’s a fancy way of saying you borrow the smartness of your source. When you say “Dr. Smith from Harvard says X,” your audience thinks you are smart because you’re connected to Dr. Smith and Harvard.

If you mess up the citation, that connection breaks. Instead of sounding like a scholar, you sound unsure.

‚ùå The Common Mumble Mistakes

I’ve watched literally hundreds of student speeches, and here’s the kind of citation slop that kills your momentum:

  • The Vague-Bomb: “A recent study found…” (Which study? From when? By who?)
  • The End-of-Sentence Dump: “The average cost is $\$50,000$, according to the June 2024 issue of Finance Monthly by writer John Doe.” (Too much info, too fast, all at the end.)
  • The Unnecessary Jargon: “As per the APA style guide’s 7th edition, chapter 5…” (NO ONE CARES about the edition during your talk.)

You’re a person talking to other people. You’re not a footnote generator. The goal is to make the audience trust you, not quiz them on your research methods.

The Power Formula: Source/Date/Qualification (And Why It Works)

The secret to a great oral citation is breaking it down and putting the most important parts first. Think of it like a quick, powerful movie trailer for your information.

Forget trying to remember every rule from MLA, APA, or Chicago style. We’re adapting them for your ears, not a dusty bibliography. You need just three things, and you can say them in any order that sounds good:

  1. Source: The author or the organization (the who).
  2. Date: The publication date (the when).
  3. Qualification: Why the source matters (the what or where).

The Simple “Source/Date/Qualification” Script

Here‚Äôs the breakdown that makes you sound totally legit:

Element Example Why It’s Powerful
Source “According to Dr. Lena Khan, a climate scientist…” The “who” gives instant authority.
Date “…in her 2023 article…” The “when” proves it’s recent info.
Qualification “…published in the Journal of Environmental Policy,” The “where” cements the credibility.

Seamless Before & After Example:

  • Before (The Awkward Mumble): “It costs about twelve thousand dollars, according to a recent thing published in the Wall Street Journal in March.”
  • After (The Seamless Pro): “The Wall Street Journal reported in March that the average cost has spiked to over $\$12,000$.”

See the difference? The “After” example leads with the authority and then drops the information naturally. The authority supports the point, it doesn’t interrupt it.

üí° Advanced Tip: Placing the Expert’s Name for Impact

This is a little debate trick I picked up from watching championship speeches. The placement of the expert’s name changes the rhetorical effect.

1. Initial Placement (When you need instant authority)

You use this when your point is kind of unbelievable or controversial, and you need to bring in the big guns right away.

  • Script: “Dr. Marcus Holloway, a Nobel-winning economist, argues that…”
  • Effect: Your audience pauses. “Wait, a Nobel winner? I better listen.” You instantly borrow his authority. This is great for opening a new, high-stakes claim.

2. Final Placement (When the data speaks for itself)

You use this when you want to hit them with the statistic first, then confirm it with the source.

  • Script: “Child obesity rates have actually dropped by $15\%$ in the last decade, according to the CDC’s latest report.”
  • Effect: The data hits them first. The source confirms the shock. This works well when your numbers are the main punch.

What About Multiple Authors?

Here’s a tip: Don’t list all three authors. Seriously. Your audience will tune out the second you say “Miller, Chang, and Papadopoulos.”

Just say the main idea and use a shortened citation: “A team of researchers from the Mayo Clinic concluded…” That’s all you need. If you’re using multiple different articles by the same people, you can say, “That same Mayo Clinic team also found…” Simple!

What to Do When the Author’s Name is Impossible

Let’s be honest. Some academic names are incredibly complex or hard to pronounce, and you do not want to stumble mid-speech. Plus, sometimes the author isn’t the key‚Äîthe organization is.

Here’s the alternative: When in doubt, use the organization or the publication.

When the Source is… Say This Instead Why?
An individual researcher. The author’s name and title. If they’re famous, use the name.
A complex author name. “A new study published in Nature Magazine…” Focus on the trusted journal/magazine.
A government document. “The U.S. Department of Education reported last year…” The department is the ultimate authority.
A website/blog. “Forbes magazine reported…” or “In an article on The Verge…” Name the publication, skip the author unless they’re super famous.

The key is to give the audience a name they trust, even if it’s the name of a reputable magazine instead of Dr. Xylophone. It still proves your point and builds that vital credibility.

Stop reading the citations. Start saying them like you’re just dropping some cool new fact you heard from an expert friend.
