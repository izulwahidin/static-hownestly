The Complete Lyrics to How Great Thou Art: Every Verse & Chorus

There are some songs that just hit different. And then there’s “How Great Thou Art,” a hymn so universally beloved, it feels less like a song and more like a shared spiritual experience for millions around the globe. It’s a powerhouse, isn’t it? From its humble Swedish folk song origins to becoming a staple in countless churches and concert halls, its power to move hearts remains undimmed.

You’re here because you want the full scoop, every single word, to truly soak in the profound journey this hymn takes you on. Maybe you want to sing along, study the poetic imagery, or just finally get those few lines you always mumble quite right. Whatever your reason, we’ve got you covered with the complete, accurate “How Great Thou Art” lyrics, carefully laid out, so you can connect with every powerful phrase.

Verse 1: God’s Majesty in Creation

The hymn kicks off with a breathtaking sweep, inviting you to join the narrator in awe as they gaze upon the vastness of creation. It’s not just about seeing; it’s about considering and understanding the intricate beauty and immense power behind every star, every storm, every corner of the universe. This opening verse sets the stage, pulling you into a reflective moment before the declaration of praise even begins.

O Lord my God, when I in awesome wonder Consider all the worlds Thy hands have made; I see the stars, I hear the mighty thunder, Thy power throughout the universe displayed.

Chorus: A Declaration of Praise

And here’s the kicker, the part everyone knows and loves. After that moment of silent, awesome wonder, the soul simply can’t help but burst forth in praise. This chorus isn’t just a catchy refrain; it’s the heart of the hymn, a powerful, repeating declaration that connects all the thematic dots. It reminds us why we’re reflecting in the first place: to lift our voices and proclaim God’s incredible greatness.

Then sings my soul, my Saviour God, to Thee, How great Thou art, how great Thou art! Then sings my soul, my Saviour God, to Thee, How great Thou art, how great Thou art!

Verse 2: The Wonders of Nature and Sky

Building on the first verse’s grand scale, the second verse brings the awe a little closer to home, focusing on the beauty found in nature’s smaller, more intimate details. We’re invited to wander through woods, hear birdsong, gaze from mountain peaks, and feel the gentle breeze. It’s a reminder that God’s majesty isn’t just in exploding galaxies; it’s right there, whispering in the rustling leaves and singing in the trees, ready for you to discover.

When through the woods and forest glades I wander, And hear the birds sing sweetly in the trees; When I look down from lofty mountain grandeur And hear the brook and feel the gentle breeze.

Verse 3: Reflecting on Christ’s Sacrifice

Plot twist! Just when you think it’s all about beautiful landscapes, the hymn takes a profound turn, shifting from creation to redemption. This verse is incredibly personal and deeply moving, focusing on Christ’s ultimate sacrifice. It’s a stark reminder of the depth of God’s love – that He would send His Son to bear our burdens and die on the cross for our sins. It connects the “how great Thou art” directly to the incredible act of salvation.

And when I think that God, His Son not sparing, Sent Him to die, I scarce can take it in; That on the cross, my burden gladly bearing, He bled and died to take away my sin.

Verse 4: The Hope of Christ’s Return

The final verse looks forward, filled with anticipation and hope. It brings the narrative full circle, moving from past creation and redemption to future glory. This isn’t just a wistful wish; it’s a confident expectation of Christ’s return and the joy that will fill believers’ hearts. When that day comes, the response will be nothing short of humble adoration, a triumphant proclamation that, once again, underscores the eternal greatness of God.

When Christ shall come with shout of acclamation And take me home, what joy shall fill my heart! Then I shall bow in humble adoration And there proclaim, my God, how great Thou art!

The Inspiring History and Origin of How Great Thou Art

Ever wonder where that incredibly powerful hymn came from? The journey of How Great Thou Art isn’t just some dusty old tale; it’s a global epic with more twists than your average Netflix binge. And no, it didn’t just magically appear in English one day.

From a Swedish Poem to a Global Hymn

Before How Great Thou Art was, you know, How Great Thou Art, it was an 1885 Swedish poem called ‘O Store Gud’ (Oh, Great God). Penned by Carl Boberg, it was a guy reflecting on a thunderstorm and a church bell. Talk about inspiration striking! From there, it started its international journey, getting translated into German and Russian.

Stuart K. Hine’s Pivotal English Translation

So, how did this Swedish gem finally land in English? Enter Stuart K. Hine, a British missionary with a knack for not just translating words, but capturing their soul. He didn’t just sit down with a dictionary; he was living and breathing the message in Ukraine, absorbing local folk melodies and crafting the verses of the How Great Thou Art we know. It wasn’t a quick gig; it was a decades-long labor of love, pulling in lines from different translations he encountered on his travels. Turns out, iconic hymns take more than a quick copy-paste.

Billy Graham’s Crusade and its Rise to Prominence

Now, for the plot twist that catapulted How Great Thou Art into global superstardom. While Hine did the heavy lifting, it was the booming voice of George Beverly Shea, singing at Billy Graham’s crusades, that made it a household staple. Imagine millions hearing it, night after night, amplified by the passionate messages of Billy Graham himself. It wasn’t just a song anymore; it was an experience. Others definitely helped spread the word, but those crusades? They were the rocket fuel.

But wait, it gets better. “How Great Thou Art” pivots to the gospel: redemption. When it mentions “the Son of God from heaven I sent Him” and “He bled and died to take away my sin,” you’re getting John 3:16 and Romans 5:8. It’s a concise, powerful theological summary of grace, musically delivered.

Hope of Eternity and Christ’s Return (Revelation 1:7)

And finally, the grand finale: the hope of eternity. The hymn anticipates Christ’s return, painting a future where we “bow in humble adoration, and there proclaim, ‘My God, how great Thou art!'” This isn’t just wishful thinking; it’s directly from Revelation 1:7, a universal promise. It’s why this hymn resonates everywhere, offering tangible hope.
