The 5-Minute Flash Freeze That Prevents Peach-Block Syndrome

The common industry myth is that you can slice peaches, dump them into a freezer bag, and call it a day. That, my friends, is how you make a frustrating 5-pound peach paperweight. The fix is a non-negotiable step that takes five minutes of prep and four hours of passive freezing.

Here is the straightforward method for how to freeze fresh peaches properly:

  • The Single Layer Rule: Arrange your prepared peach slices (or halves) in a single, non-overlapping layer across the parchment. If the pieces touch, they’ll freeze together. Don’t be greedy; use a second tray.
  • The Flash Freeze: Place the tray(s) into the freezer for 1 to 4 hours.
  • The Visual Cue: The moment they are solid to the touch and cannot be easily pressed together, they are ready. They should sound like little pebbles when you gently shuffle them.

If your homemade frozen peaches look like something you dug out of an archaeological site‚Äîa depressing, muddy brown‚Äîyou‚Äôre dealing with oxidation. You have three professional-grade options:

  • Lemon Juice: The kitchen workhorse. It’s citric acid, readily available, and effective. The precise, safe formula is 1 tablespoon of fresh lemon juice per cup of cold water. This is enough to drop the $\text{pH}$ and halt the browning without giving your peaches a jarringly tart flavor.
  • The Sugar Pack: This is a secondary, less-effective anti-browning agent, and it only works by limiting the fruit’s exposure to oxygen, not by halting the enzyme itself. You simply toss the sliced peaches in sugar (about 1/2 cup per 4 cups of sliced peaches).
