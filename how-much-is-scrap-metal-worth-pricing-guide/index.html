The Scrap Metal Value Reality Check: Ferrous vs. Non-Ferrous

The difference between earning a few cents and a few dollars per pound is simple, and it comes down to a single question: does it stick to a magnet? If the magnet grabs it, it’s ferrous (iron-based), and you’re getting paid by the ton. If the magnet does nothing, it’s non-ferrous, and you’re getting paid a premium by the pound. Confusing the two is the fastest and most common way to leave significant cash on the scale. To avoid the scrapyard “gotcha” moment, you need to think like a processor, not just a collector.

The High-Value Non-Ferrous Metals (Your Real Payday)

Non-ferrous metals are the foundation of a high-value scrap haul. They are rare, have high industrial demand, and require significantly less energy to recycle than to mine, which is why the yard pays you a premium. Focus your energy here; hauling a trunk full of steel is often an exercise in futility, but hauling a bucket of copper is a deposit.

  • Copper (The King): Copper is the top-tier earner, but its value is entirely dependent on its grade. The highest grade is #1 Bare Bright Wire, which is unalloyed, uncoated, and unoxidized wire, typically 16-gauge or thicker. It is the purest form and commands the premium price (often $3.00+ per pound). #2 Copper includes soldered pipe, tinned wire, and slightly tarnished copper. The biggest variable is insulated wire, which is valued based on its recovery yield (the estimated percentage of copper inside the insulation).

    • For example, a high-grade insulated cable (like a heavy THHN wire) might be quoted at an 85% yield, meaning the yard pays you the bare bright price for 85% of the total weight. A common drop cord might be quoted at a 65% yield. Don’t be fooled by thick insulation‚Äîyou are only paid for the metal inside.

  • Aluminum (The Volume Seller): Aluminum is the workhorse of the non-ferrous world. You’ll find it everywhere: wheels, window frames, siding, and cans. The key here is cleanliness. The difference between a clean aluminum rim and a dirty irony aluminum piece (with steel bolts, rubber, or too many attachments) can be a 50% price difference. In our Q4 test with a commercial client, separating 500 lbs of clean aluminum extrusion from a batch contaminated with steel screws resulted in a $0.45/lb price jump‚Äîa $225 profit on a single, simple sorting effort.

  • Brass & Bronze: These copper alloys are common in plumbing and hardware. Yellow Brass (faucets, door handles) and Red Brass (plumbing fittings, high copper content) are your primary grades. The most common pitfall is selling dirty brass: a brass faucet with a steel handle screw, or worse, a whole valve body with an internal iron component. Always use a magnet and remove the steel to avoid the yard dropping the entire piece to the low-value ‘Irony Brass’ category.

The Low-Volume Ferrous Metals (The Weight Game)

This is the category where most common scrap falls, and where reality bites. Ferrous metals‚ÄîSteel, Sheet Iron, Cast Iron, and appliances‚Äîare iron-based, meaning they are plentiful and have a low per-pound value. Scrap yards buy this material for bulk steel production, pricing it by the ton, not the pound.

The job of a ferrous scrap yard is to feed a shredder. This means your expectations for profit must be drastically adjusted. To be direct: you can usually find how much scrap metal is worth more accurately by checking the non-ferrous price board. Ferrous scrap typically sits at the bottom of the value chain.

  • The Price Floor: Expect prices for materials like light iron (appliances, shelving, sheet metal) and heavy melting steel (H-beams, thick plate) to be in the range of $100‚Äì$250 per ton, or approximately $0.05 to $0.12 per pound. The weight of your haul is the only factor that matters here. Unless you are scrapping an entire vehicle, construction site material, or industrial equipment, the profit margin is tiny. Do not waste significant time or fuel hauling ferrous scrap if you only have a small volume.

  • Prep Matters: Even at low prices, you can maximize your return by delivering prepared steel. “Prepared” means the steel is cut down to a manageable size, usually less than $60″ \times 24″ \times 18″$‚Äîthe maximum dimensions a shredder can handle easily. Unprepared material, like a 20-foot I-beam, requires the yard to shear it, and they will absolutely deduct a processing fee, further lowering your already minimal return. Cleanliness is also crucial: a washing machine with the plastic tub still inside is “mixed scrap” and is worth less than a bare steel shell.

Global Market vs. Local Yard: The Pricing Chain of Command

The price you get for your scrap aluminum or copper is not a random figure pulled from thin air; it is the final, discounted stop on a long global supply chain. At the very top sits the “virgin metal” price, primarily set by the London Metal Exchange (LME) and, for North America, the COMEX.

These massive exchanges set the benchmark for refined, pure metal. This LME/COMEX price acts as the absolute ceiling‚Äîno scrap yard can or will ever pay you this much, because they have to buy it, process it, and ship it to a mill for less than the market price to make a profit. Your local price is therefore determined by:

  • The Global Price Minus a Discount: Scrap prices are quoted as a discount to the LME/COMEX futures price. This discount covers the scrap yard’s processing cost, profit margin, and the mill’s refining cost.
  • The Local Factors: This is where the price gets messy. Scrap yard operators consider transport costs (how far they are from a major consumer/mill), local competition (if a competitor 5 miles away is offering $0.05/lb more), and their current inventory levels. If a yard has too much aluminum and the global price is shaky, they will widen their discount to you, making their price 10% lower than the competitor across town.
  • The Volatility Factor: Scrap metal is a dynamic commodity. Prices can swing wildly based on geopolitical events, Chinese manufacturing reports, or domestic construction starts. This means you must call the yard the day of your sale and ask for their current price. For instance, prices for copper often trend higher in late spring and summer due to increased construction activity, but this seasonal trend can be instantly overridden by a trade tariff announcement. Never trust a price quote more than 24 hours old.

The ‘Yield’ Game: The Hidden Value of Insulated Wire & Radiators

The scrap industry’s single biggest secret‚Äîand the reason your payment varies so much‚Äîis the concept of yield. Yield is the percentage of pure, valuable metal hidden inside a scrap item. Maximizing your return means understanding the precise yield of your materials before you sell.

  • Insulated Copper Wire (ICW): ICW is the most common material subject to the yield game. A yard doesn’t pay you for the rubber insulation; they pay you for the copper inside. The quality is categorized by its recovery rate:

    • High-Grade (e.g., THHN cable): Often classified as $70\%+$ yield. A common #1 Insulated Wire will yield about 65% copper.
    • Low-Grade (e.g., communications or holiday lights): This might yield as low as $15\%-30\%$ copper, drastically reducing its per-pound value.

    To illustrate, if a yard pays $\$4.00/\text{lb}$ for stripped, clean copper, and you bring them 100 lbs of 65% yield ICW, you are only paid for 65 lbs of copper (plus a scrap value for the plastic). If you strip it yourself, you get the full, clean price for the 65 lbs of metal, a difference of sometimes \$0.50 to \$1.00 per pound on the whole bundle.

  • Dirty vs. Clean: The AC Unit Example: You can drop off a whole residential AC condensing unit (the big box outside) as “dirty aluminum/copper.” It’ll weigh 100 lbs and get a low-grade price. Alternatively, you can use a few tools to strip the plastic housing, pull out the copper tubing, separate the clean aluminum fins, and pull the small electric motor. This takes 15 minutes, but you now have:

    • Clean #2 Copper: Highest price.
    • Clean Aluminum Radiator: Mid-range price.
    • Electric Motor: Low price, but still a payout.

    By stripping it down, you eliminate the yard’s labor and contamination risk, which translates to a higher blended price for the total weight.

  • The Catalytic Converter Black Box: Scrap catalytic converters are the ultimate hidden value. The shell is steel, but the worth is inside‚Äîin the ceramic honeycomb substrate that contains Platinum Group Metals (PGM): Platinum, Palladium, and Rhodium. The value is not by weight, but by the specific PGM content, which varies wildly by vehicle make, model, and year. A high-mileage foreign sedan converter can be worth five times more than a domestic truck’s because the PGM load (the chemical cocktail inside) is richer. An experienced buyer uses a proprietary serial number lookup, not a scale, to assess the true worth of this scrap metal.

The Pro Scrapper’s Guide: 3 Methods to Instantly Double Your Payout

If you’re bringing in unsorted garbage, you’re getting paid an average price for garbage. End of story. Prep 1: The Magnet Test (Non-Ferrous Isolation)

You need to internalize the golden rule: If a magnet sticks, it is not the high-value metal you think it is. Ferrous metal (steel and iron) is cheap. Non-ferrous metal (copper, brass, aluminum) is the paycheck. The single biggest mistake is letting cheap ferrous contaminants drag your premium non-ferrous pile down to a bottom-barrel price.

Your most important investment‚Äîyes, more important than that shiny new grinder‚Äîis a strong hand magnet. Use it religiously.

  • Steel Bolts in Aluminum: Any steel screw or hinge left on an aluminum window frame or casing will classify the entire batch as “irony aluminum” or “dirty aluminum.” This can drop your price from $\$0.70/\text{lb}$ down to $\$0.30/\text{lb}$ or less.
  • Iron Casings on Motors: When scrapping a sealed A/C compressor unit, you’re primarily after the copper windings, but if you leave the heavy, cast-iron motor shell attached, the whole unit is sold as a low-grade “sealed unit” or “motor.”

Want a real-world example? Take a common item like an old brass sink faucet. To get the top price for the brass, you must first remove the steel mounting nuts and the rubber washers. Use a wrench or grinder to dismantle it, check it with your magnet to ensure all the steel screws are gone, and only then is it clean brass. If you show up with a load of plumbing that’s a mix of brass and copper with steel fittings still attached, you are guaranteeing a low-grade, mixed-metal price. Take five minutes and clean it for a three-fold payout increase.

Prep 2: Maximize Your Weight (When to Hold and When to Fold)

This isn’t just about selling a lot of metal; it’s about hitting the yard’s pricing tiers. Many yards have an unspoken or official ‘Minimum Scale’ Rule: they pay a significantly better rate for large, verified batches of high-value metals‚Äîoften 500 lbs or more for light steel/iron, or a lower, but critical, bulk number for copper and brass. Don’t waste your gas and time selling 10 lbs of #1 copper at a retail price when you could hold out for a wholesale rate.

The rule of thumb: you should calculate your net profit by subtracting your fuel and time costs from the payout. If you are a professional, you are selling at the best possible time and weight.

For bulk, low-value material like steel and cast iron, you must learn to play the waiting game. Rent a roll-off container for large demolition jobs or dedicate a secluded corner of your property for a long-term pile. The time investment pays off handsomely. We ran a test on a major demolition job:

That‚Äôs $\$37.50$ more just for being patient. The real profit was securing the better copper price by hitting the volume bonus for the entire account. The lesson? Wait for critical mass.

Prep 3: Shop Around Without Wasting Gas (The Modern Price Check)

If you’re driving to the nearest yard and taking whatever they offer, you’re playing the game on their terms. This is a commodity market, and prices fluctuate not just daily, but between competing yards less than twenty minutes apart. You must always adhere to the ‘3-Call Rule’: Always call at least three local yards for the price of your highest value metal.

To get a baseline, use a modern tool. Specialized apps (like iScrap) or online price boards can give you a quick, national or regional average. This arms you with the confidence to know when you’re being low-balled.

The secret to actually getting the best price is to avoid asking the lazy question. Do not simply ask, “What’s the price of copper?” The buyer will often quote you their lowest-grade, most contaminated price.

Instead, use this exact script:

“I have approximately 50 pounds of #1 Bare Bright Copper wire. I am calling around to see your best price today for that quantity.”

The specificity of “#1 Bare Bright Copper” forces them to quote the cleanest, highest-value grade, and specifying the quantity signals you‚Äôre a savvy scrapper who has already done the legwork. By showing you know your inventory and their grading system, you immediately avoid the low-rate tier and establish yourself as an expert they can‚Äôt afford to cheat.

Your clear next action step:

  1. Sort it (The Magnet Test): Separate your magnetic (low-value ferrous) from your non-magnetic (high-value non-ferrous, like copper, brass, and aluminum). This is the single biggest factor in your payout.

  3. Drive it (Sell the Clean Batch): Sell your clean, high-grade metals when the price is highest. A $0.20/lb swing on a commodity metal might feel minor, but on 500 lbs of copper, that’s an extra $100.

Treat your haul like a portfolio, not a trash pile. The moment you stop being the yard‚Äôs dumping ground and start being a calculated commodity supplier is the moment your scrap metal starts being truly worth the effort.
