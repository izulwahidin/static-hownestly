The Thirteen Ceremonial Triangle Folds

  1. First Fold (Triangle Start): The person at the striped end starts by folding the striped corner (the end opposite the stars) over to form a triangle. The open edge of the triangle should be parallel to the long edge of the folded flag.

  • Step 2: The Second Lengthwise Fold. Repeat the action. Bring the freshly folded edge up to meet the original top edge of the flag. Troubleshooting the Fold: Why Your Triangle Won’t Close (And How to Fix It)

If you’ve followed the steps perfectly and the final triangle looks more like a crumpled napkin than a crisp tribute, you’ve stumbled on the most common frustration. It’s not a secret military handshake you’re missing; it’s almost always a matter of precision in the very first steps.

  • Problem: You reach the end and the long strip of material that needs to be tucked in simply won’t reach or there’s too much slack.
    • Solution: You made your initial lengthwise folds too generous. Undo the fold and make the two lengthwise folds (the ones that hide the red and white stripes) significantly tighter and smaller.   • Problem: The final triangle is sloppy, loose, or fails to hold a rigid shape.
    • Solution: You didn’t execute a crisp enough 90-degree turn on every subsequent fold. Every single triangular fold should be treated like a hard crease, ensuring the bottom of the triangle is perfectly parallel to the top edge before folding. When to call it quits: There’s no shame in admitting that some flags are simply too large or too damaged for the crisp triangle. At that point, you have two respectable options: either execute the best, tightest rectangular fold possible, or, if the flag is damaged beyond repair or dirty, find a way to respectfully dispose of it.
