The Parking Procedure That Saves Your Transmission (The N-EPB-P Sequence)

Forget what you learned about old-school handbrakes. The electronic system is simple, but the order of operations is critical. This specific N-EPB-P sequence is the only way to ensure the Subaru Forester 2025 premium‚Äôs weight rests squarely on the brake pads via the Electronic Parking Brake (EPB) and not the delicate parking pawl inside your CVT transmission. Step-by-Step: Engaging the Electronic Parking Brake (EPB) Properly

This isn’t an optional ritual; it’s the procedure that isolates the vehicle’s weight from the transmission‚Äôs parking pawl‚Äîa critical step, particularly on any incline. If you jump straight from Drive to Park and then engage the EPB, you’ve already stressed the transmission. The key is to engage the brake before shifting into Park.

Here is the correct, transmission-saving sequence:

  1. Stop Completely and Hold: Bring the Forester to a complete stop and keep your foot firmly pressed on the main foot brake.
  2. Shift to Neutral (N): This is the non-negotiable step. Shifting into Neutral momentarily disconnects the engine/CVT from the wheels, allowing the vehicle to roll slightly, if necessary, before the parking brake is applied.
  3. Engage the EPB: Pull up on the electronic parking brake switch, located near the shifter console, until the red indicator light illuminates steadily.
  4. Settle the Vehicle: Release the foot brake while still in Neutral (N). You will feel or hear a slight ‘settling’ as the vehicle‚Äôs weight transfers fully onto the engaged Electronic Parking Brake. This confirms the load is off the drivetrain.
  5. Shift to Park (P) and Shut Down: Only now should you shift to Park (P) and turn off the ignition.

By following this N-EPB-P sequence, you ensure the transmission is completely unstressed every time you park, regardless of the incline.

Releasing the EPB: How the ‘Auto’ Function Works on the 2025 Forester

The 2025 Subaru Forester 2025 premium is smart enough to handle most of the release procedure for you, a welcome departure from the manual routine of lesser vehicles. However, the system is designed to be safe, which means it has specific, non-negotiable conditions for auto-release.

The Electronic Parking Brake will automatically disengage and save you a button push, but only if the following three conditions are simultaneously met:

Myth Busted: Why ‘Auto Vehicle Hold’ (AVH) Is Not the Parking Brake

This is where most owners get confused and start complaining about the car “holding” at stoplights. Auto Vehicle Hold (AVH) is a convenience feature for traffic, not a replacement for the parking brake. Think of it as a helpful, slightly overzealous assistant‚Äîit’s not the primary security guard.

The biggest mistake new owners make is assuming AVH is the same thing as setting the park brake, leading to the frantic question, “How do I turn this thing off?” You need to understand the difference between a temporary pressure holder and a mechanical lock. This isn’t rocket science, but ignoring the distinction will lead to unnecessary wear and tear, or worse, a rolling vehicle.

AVH vs. EPB: What They Do (And What They Don’t)

The two systems exist side-by-side, controlled by switches often located in the same console area near the shifter. They are activated by physically pressing a button (or pulling a lever for the EPB), but their function is fundamentally different. If you treat them as interchangeable, you‚Äôve missed the point entirely.

Auto Vehicle Hold (AVH) is a hydraulic helper designed for drive-throughs and stop-and-go traffic.

  • Function: It holds the pressure you applied to the brake pedal when the vehicle is stopped in Drive ($D$) or Reverse ($R$). It keeps the car stationary so you can take your foot off the pedal without shifting to Park ($P$).
  • Release: The second you lightly touch the accelerator pedal, the system releases the pressure instantly, and the car moves.
  • Dash Indicator: You‚Äôll see the “HOLD” indicator illuminated in green (when active) or amber (when ready to activate).

The Electronic Parking Brake (EPB), by contrast, is a mechanical safety lock.

  • Function: This is a true parking brake. It uses an electric motor to engage the rear brake calipers, providing a mechanical lock on the wheels. It’s intended for long-term stops, parking on an incline, or as an emergency brake.
  • Release: It releases only when you press the button (or pull the lever) while the brake pedal is depressed and the ignition is on.
  • Dash Indicator: A solid red circle with a P in the middle ($(\text{P})$) or a red indicator light will illuminate when the parking brake is engaged.

The button to turn AVH on or off is typically located right next to the EPB switch. Engaging AVH is a set-it-and-forget-it feature for the drive cycle, but it is not a substitute for putting the car in $P$ and engaging the EPB when you exit the vehicle. The table below breaks down the reality:

The Honest Truth: When to Turn Off Auto Vehicle Hold

The AVH is brilliant 90% of the time, especially in dense urban traffic, saving your leg from that constant foot-on-the-brake dance. But like any automated feature, it has blind spots, and recognizing them is a matter of driver competence and safety. Don’t be the driver who curses the technology because they didn’t understand the edge cases.

There are specific, common scenarios where the AVH function can become genuinely annoying or even mildly dangerous:

  • Tight Parallel Parking: If you rely on the car‚Äôs natural “creep” (the slow, un-accelerated movement a car makes in $D$ or $R$), AVH will prevent it, forcing you to use the accelerator for every tiny adjustment. This makes millimeter-accurate parking harder than it needs to be.
  • Drive-Up Window Shuffling: In scenarios where you need to inch forward repeatedly, like a busy coffee shop line, the constant engagement and disengagement of AVH can feel jerky and unnatural. Switch it off for a smoother roll.

The AVH feature directly affects the “creep” factor‚Äîthe slight forward motion that a conventional automatic transmission provides at idle. If you’re maneuvering in a space requiring a smooth, slow, and un-accelerated roll, the abrupt engagement of AVH when you lift your foot will interrupt that flow, forcing you to tap the gas and brakes instead of simply modulating the pedal pressure.

For those worried about rollbacks on hills, remember that’s what the dedicated Hill Holder Assist is for.
