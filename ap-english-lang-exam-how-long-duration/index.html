The Multi-Guessing Game: Timing the 45-Question Multiple-Choice Section

You have exactly 60 minutes to complete 45 multiple-choice questions. Do the quick math: that gives you roughly 1 minute and 20 seconds per question. Before you panic, understand this is an average, and you are not a machine. Some discrete questions about a single sentence will take 30 seconds; a dense passage analysis might take 2 minutes.

Your primary directive in this section is ruthless efficiency. Do not spend more than 2 minutes on any single passage or question set. If you are struggling to find the best answer out of four plausible options, it’s time to mark your best guess and move on. Wasting 3 minutes on one question means sacrificing the time you needed for two others. The skills tested here‚Äîunderstanding the Rhetorical Situation, identifying Claims & Evidence, and analyzing Reasoning & Organization‚Äîare all predicated on quick, precise reading, not deep academic reflection.

Here is your Pacing Cheat Sheet for when to bail on a tough question:

  • 1:45 Mark: You’ve narrowed it down to two choices, but can‚Äôt find the specific textual evidence to eliminate one. Make a calculated guess.
  • 2:00 Mark: You‚Äôre on the third read of the question, and the answer choices still feel like they were written in ancient Greek. Mark the one that is least wrong, circle the question in your booklet, and move on immediately. If you finish the section early, this is the only question you go back to.
  • Your Mental Check: If you feel an internal sigh of frustration or a spike of panic, your time-to-value ratio has just dipped into the red. Stop, guess, and go. A 75% guess is better than a 100% missed opportunity later.

Synthesis Essay: The Counterintuitive 15/20/10 Split That Wins

The Synthesis essay is the only essay on the AP Lang exam that gives you a guaranteed, separate reading period: 15 full minutes to examine the sources. Most students treat this as a nice break before the writing, but that is a rookie mistake. This 15-minute window is the single most valuable asset you have on the test.

Here‚Äôs the winning strategy: Spend more time here.

  • 15 Minutes: This is your outlining period. Use this time not just to read, but to actively plot your argument, determine your thesis, and, crucially, pre-select which sources will support which point. Don’t waste time on a separate outline later.
  • 20 Minutes: Dive immediately into the writing. Your pre-work means you are just executing the argument. Focus on integrating the sources seamlessly (e.g., “Source B, which details the economic impact, confirms this concern…”).
  • 10 Minutes: Revision and polish. Check for source attribution errors and ensure your body paragraphs directly support your thesis.

Why this works: The Synthesis prompt is fundamentally different from the Argument prompt because you are not debating a topic; you are establishing a position using provided evidence. The Argument essay, by contrast, tests your internal knowledge bank. By front-loading the organization into the free 15 minutes, you essentially turn a 45-minute writing sprint into a much more relaxed 30-minute drafting process. If you can make a clear claim and use 4-5 sources effectively, you‚Äôve already won the points your peers lose by rushing their reading.

Rhetorical Analysis Essay: Finding the Sweet Spot Between Detail and Development

The Rhetorical Analysis essay is the place where you must resist the urge to simply summarize the text. You are not a glorified book report writer; you are a detective analyzing an author’s strategy. Your focus is not what the author said, but why they said it that way, and what effect that choice was intended to have on the audience. This is where you demonstrate the high-level critical thinking the AP readers are grading for.

Your pacing advice: Stop trying to analyze every rhetorical device you see. Instead, follow the Rule of Three:

  1. 5-7 Minutes: Analyze the prompt and text for 3 key rhetorical moves. These should be large, structural, or strategic choices, not just isolated examples of alliteration or hyperbole. For instance, focus on an appeal to pathos, a structural shift from personal anecdote to statistical data, and a final call to action built on parallelism.
  2. 35-40 Minutes: Write a powerful body paragraph dedicated to each of those three moves. Your paragraph structure should be:
    • Topic Sentence: Claim about the author’s choice.
    • Evidence: A specific quote or detail from the text.
    • Commentary: The why and the effect. This is the gold.

I’ll admit it: finishing a well-developed Rhetorical Analysis in 40 minutes is tough, especially if the text is complex. However, it is necessary. If you spend 55 minutes here, you compromise your score on the Argument essay, which is the final sprint. A slightly shorter, but perfectly structured and deeply analyzed Rhetorical Analysis will consistently score higher than a longer one that simply summarizes the author’s message. Focus on quality of insight over quantity of devices.

Argument Essay: The 5-Minute Brainstorm That Saves Your Score

The final leg of the marathon is the Argument essay, and it presents a unique challenge: the evidence comes entirely from your head. This is why the first five minutes are the most critical. You can write a perfect thesis, but if you back it up with vague, poorly-developed examples, your score will collapse.

The Pacing Strategy:

  • 5 Minutes: This is non-negotiable brainstorming time. Your goal is to generate 3 concrete, specific, and varied examples to support your thesis. “Varied” means pulling from different categories: a piece of well-known historical evidence, a specific literary example, and a detailed, relevant personal anecdote or observation. Specificity is the key. Don’t just say, “The Civil War.” Say, “Lincoln‚Äôs Second Inaugural Address, where he appealed to national unity post-conflict…”
  • 40 Minutes: Write the essay. Here‚Äôs the expert tip that separates the 4s from the 5s: Don’t waste time on a perfect introduction. Write a strong, clear, and debatable thesis statement in 1-2 sentences and dive immediately into your first body paragraph.

In our internal score analysis, we had one student who spent a disproportionate amount of time (20 minutes) on a gorgeous, witty introduction for the Argument essay, leaving only 25 minutes for the evidence. Her peer, who wrote a 2-sentence intro and spent the next 43 minutes providing three deeply-developed, highly specific examples‚Äîciting everything from the development of the polio vaccine to a specific legislative bill‚Äîscored a 5. The takeaway is clear: the AP Lang rubric prioritizes the Quality of Evidence and its Commentary over flowery, five-paragraph-essay structure. Get to your evidence. Your time is a finite resource; spend it on the things that actually earn the points.
