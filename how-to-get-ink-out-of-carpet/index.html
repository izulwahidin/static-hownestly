Is it Water-Based Ink? (Think Washable Markers or Fountain Pens)

These are your easiest cleanup jobs, thank goodness. Water-based ink is designed to dissolve with water, which is a massive win for your carpet.

  • How to tell: It probably smeared or ran a little when it first hit the carpet. Kids’ washable markers are the classic example.

How to Use Alcohol Without Ruining Everything

This process is all about transferring the ink from the carpet onto a clean cloth. You are not scrubbing, remember? You’re blotting.

  1. Dampen a Cloth: Grab a clean, white cloth (or a paper towel, but cloth works better). Pour some rubbing alcohol onto it until it’s wet, but not soaking or dripping. You don’t want to dump alcohol directly onto the carpet, because that can spread the stain.
  2. Start Dabbing: Press the damp cloth firmly onto the ink spot. Hold it there for a few seconds. The ink should start bleeding onto the cloth.
  3. Lift and Repeat: Lift the cloth and look at it. See that blue/black/red ink? Success! Now, switch to a clean spot on your cloth and dab the carpet again. Never use the same inky part of the cloth twice. This is critical.
  4. Keep Going: Keep dabbing, switching the cloth to a clean section every time, until no more ink transfers. This takes patience. Don’t rush it.
  5. Rinse with Water: Once the ink is gone, dampen a new clean cloth with just water. Blot the area to rinse out the alcohol residue. You don’t want to leave that behind.
  6. Blot Dry: Press the area with a dry towel until all the moisture is gone.

Quick Tip: Always, always test the rubbing alcohol on a hidden piece of carpet first (like in a closet). This makes sure it won’t strip the color out of your nice rug. You don’t want a clean spot that’s also bleached white.

üçã Section 1: The Gross Profit Margin (The Cost of Goods)

This is the first, fastest check on your product or service. The gross profit margin tells you how much money you keep after paying the direct costs of making or delivering what you sell.

$$\text{Gross Margin} = \frac{\text{Revenue} – \text{COGS}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100$$

So, if you made $10,000 in sales and your COGS was $4,000, your Gross Profit is $6,000. Divide $6,000 by $10,000, and you get 0.6.

üè¶ Section 2: The Net Profit Margin (The Real Boss)

  1. Figure out your Net Profit: Take your Gross Profit and subtract all your Operating Expenses. Don’t forget interest and taxes!
  2. Turn it into a Percentage: Divide that Net Profit by the Total Revenue. Again, multiply by 100.

$$\text{Net Margin} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100$$
