The Standard Way: Two Strap Buttons (The Easy Life)

If you have an electric guitar or an acoustic that was built in the last 20 years, you probably have two of these little metal knobs, called strap buttons. This is the best-case scenario. It’s basically a plug-and-play situation.

Where Are the Strap Buttons?

First things first, you need to find them.

  • The Bottom Button: This one is always super easy to spot. It’s on the very bottom edge of the guitar’s body, right in the center. It’s the one that stares straight at your knee when you’re playing sitting down.
  • The Top Button: This one is the trickiest to find because it moves around depending on the guitar type.
    • Electric Guitars: It‚Äôs usually on the upper “horn” (that pointy bit on the top side of the guitar body).
    • Acoustic Guitars: It’s often on the heel of the neck, where the neck meets the body of the guitar.

Got them? Good. Now for the actual hard part‚Äîwhich isn’t even that hard.

Snapping the Strap into Place

Your strap has two holes, one on each end. They’re usually reinforced with leather or a tough plastic.

  1. Start with the Bottom: Take one end of your strap and push it onto the bottom strap button. You’ll need a little force. The key is to roll the hole over the button using your thumb. Don’t just pull straight down.
  2. Do the Top: Do the exact same rolling maneuver with the other end of the strap onto the top strap button.

It should feel super snug. If it comes off easily, you need to either push harder or get a better strap. Pro Tip: New straps are usually stiff. Wiggle and roll it on. It should take a little effort to seat it completely.

And that’s it. Hot take: If you can put a lid on a stubborn Tupperware container, you can definitely put on a guitar strap this way.

When You Only Have ONE Button (Acoustic Guitar Shenanigans)

Okay, now for the slightly more annoying situation: the classic, no-frills acoustic guitar.

Lots of older or simpler acoustic guitars only have the one strap button on the bottom. Why? Because the company saved two cents, probably. So now you have an awesome strap and nowhere to put the top part.

You have two main options here. One is cheap and old-school, the other is a little safer.

Option A: The Shoelace Method (The Classic)

This is how people did it back in the day before fancy accessories. All you need is a durable string. A bootlace, strong paracord, or the fancy string that came with your strap works great.

  1. Strap on the Bottom: Put one end of your strap onto the existing bottom strap button, just like we did before.
  2. Find the Headstock: Look up at the guitar’s headstock (where the tuning pegs are). The best spot to attach the string is behind the nut‚Äîthat little white or black ridge where the strings pass over before the tuners. You’re basically aiming for the top of the neck, right below the tuning pegs.
  3. Tie the String: Loop your shoelace or string through the empty hole on the strap’s top end. Then, wrap that string tightly around the neck, right behind the nut.
  4. Knot it Up: Tie a secure, double knot (like tying your shoe, but tighter) so the strap sits comfortably behind the neck and is stable.

Warning: This method works, but having a string tied up high can sometimes get in the way of your hand if you’re playing chords way up the neck.

Option B: The Headstock Tie or Quick-Release (The Upgrade)

If you don’t like tying knots, you can buy a little gadget specifically for this. It’s called a headstock strap tie or quick-release strap lock.

  • It’s a small leather or nylon loop.
  • The loop goes around the neck, right behind the nut (same spot as the string).
  • The other end has a little buckle or button that the strap’s hole can attach to.

Why is this better? It‚Äôs usually made of tougher material than a shoelace, and it makes it super fast to take your strap off and put it back on, which is great if you use the case a lot. It‚Äôs a cheap little item that makes your life easier.

The Important Part: Protecting Your Guitar (Strap Locks)

Here’s the quick reality check: Even when you put the strap on perfectly, it can still come loose. You’re jumping around, you’re swinging the guitar, or maybe the strap holes just got stretched out over time.

And here’s the thing… a fallen guitar is a broken guitar.

To stop this from happening, you need strap locks.

  1. What they are: They are little metal or plastic pieces that basically lock your strap to the strap button. They make it impossible for the strap to slip off, even if you do a Pete Townshend windmill.
  2. How they work: You replace your existing strap buttons with the strap lock buttons. Then, you screw the corresponding lock piece onto the strap itself. When you put them together, they literally lock with a click.

It‚Äôs like adding a seatbelt to your strap. Yes, it takes five minutes and a small screwdriver to install the buttons, but it’s the best insurance policy you can buy for your instrument. If your guitar is worth more than the cost of a large pizza, you need strap locks. Period.

The Bottom Line

So that’s the deal. Putting on a guitar strap is all about knowing where to put the two holes‚Äîon the two buttons, or one button plus a secure tie on the neck.

Now you know the trick for the standard setup and the workaround for the tricky acoustic. You’ve even got the secret knowledge about strap locks.
