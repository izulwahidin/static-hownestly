üíé The Four Cs: Why Two $70,000 Diamonds Aren’t the Same

Okay, so you‚Äôve picked your team, either natural or lab. But why is there still a price range even within those categories? That‚Äôs where the “Four Cs” come in. These are the four criteria that jewelers use to grade a diamond, and they are everything.

Cut: The Sparkle Maker

This is the most important “C,” and honestly, the one you shouldn’t skimp on. Cut refers to how well the diamond is shaped and faceted, not the stone’s physical shape (like round or oval). A perfect cut means the light bounces around inside and explodes out in a brilliant sparkle. A bad cut means the light leaks out the bottom, making the diamond look dull, even if it has perfect clarity.

  • Rule of Thumb: Always pick the highest cut grade you can afford. It makes a huge difference.

Color: Less Yellow, More Zeroes

Diamond color is graded on a scale from D (colorless, like water) to Z (noticeably yellow or brown). For a huge 4-carat rock, color matters because a tiny bit of yellow is much easier to spot.

  • The Sweet Spot: Most smart buyers aim for the G or H color grade. They look totally white to your naked eye but cost way less than the D, E, or F grades.

Clarity: The Invisible Flaws

Clarity is all about the little natural flaws, or “inclusions,” inside the diamond. Jewelers use a 10x magnification glass to check for them.

  • Flawless (FL) is perfect, but it costs a mint and is ridiculously rare.
  • VS1 or VS2 (Very Slight Inclusions) is a great choice. You save a ton of money, and the flaws are so tiny you can’t see them without a jeweler‚Äôs magnifying tool.

Carat is just the diamond’s weight. One carat is $0.2$ grams. And since a 4-carat stone is so rare, the price doesn’t just go up by four times the cost of a 1-carat stone‚Äîit goes up exponentially. That’s diamond economics for you!
