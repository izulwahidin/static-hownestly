The 4-Table, 16-Player Architecture (And How to Scale It)

If you think you can scrape by with a few random dice and napkins for scorekeeping, you’re planning a disaster. The Bunco directions require specific gear, and for good reason.

The Rolling and Scoring Engine: Target Numbers and Bunco

The entire game is a delightful, dice-fueled obsession with a single number. That number is the Target Number for the current round. In Round 1, you are targeting the ‘1s’; in Round 2, you are targeting the ‘2s’, and so on, all the way up to Round 6.

Here‚Äôs the simple, non-negotiable scoring:

  • Target Number Hit: For each die that matches the current round‚Äôs target number, you score 1 point. If you roll a 1, 2, and 6 in Round 1, you score 1 point (for the ‘1’).
  • Keep Rolling: As long as you roll at least one Target Number, you keep the dice. It’s your turn until you roll all three dice and none of them match the Target Number. That’s when you pass the dice to the opposing player on your right.

The Real Point-Getters: Bunco and Mini-Bunco

If you want to win, you’re rolling for triples.

  1. Bunco (The Jackpot): Roll three dice that all match the Target Number (e.g., three ‘3s’ in Round 3). This is an immediate, automatic, and glorious 21 points. The player who rolls it should scream a little, grab a score sheet, and continue rolling the dice until they don’t hit any Target Numbers.
  2. Mini-Bunco (The Consolation Prize): Roll three dice that match, but they do not match the current Target Number (e.g., three ‘6s’ in Round 3). This scores 5 points. Nice, but it’s no Bunco.

Signaling and Rotation: Winning the Table and Moving On

The round stops, abruptly and dramatically, thanks to the Head Table. The Head Table is simply the table designated as Table 1, and it’s their job to end the round when a team reaches a pre-determined score, typically 21 points.

When the Head Table hits 21 (or whatever score you decided on), their entire table stops playing, and one of the players (usually the designated scorekeeper) rings a bell or loudly announces the round’s end. This is the stop signal for every other table. Whatever table you are at, you must immediately stop rolling and total your score.

The Mandatory Player Migration

Once the round score has been officially tallied at every table, it’s time for the shuffle. This is the part new players usually mess up, so pay attention. This movement ensures that everyone plays with new people every round, which is the whole point of Bunco‚Äôs social genius.

Here are the precise movement rules:

  • The Head Table (Table 1): The winning partners stay at the Head Table, but the losing partners move down to the lowest available table (Table 2 if there are only 2, or Table 4/5/6 if there are more).
  • All Other Tables: The winning team stays put. The losing team moves down to the next lowest-numbered table (e.g., from Table 3 to Table 4).
  • Partner Switch: Crucially, no matter what, the High-Scoring partner and Low-Scoring partner at each winning/losing team must switch sides of the table to face new opponents (or, for the winners, continue facing the same opponents but in a different seat). If you were a winner sitting across from your partner, you’ll now sit next to them to face a different opposing team.

This rotation is non-negotiable. It keeps the game fresh, makes sure no one hogs the luck, and prevents you from playing with the same dull person all night. The rotation is complete when everyone has settled into their new seats and the dice are ready for the next Target Number.
