The Base Duration: 90 Minutes of Regulation Time

The rule is straightforward, yet universally misunderstood: per Law 7, a standard adult soccer match is comprised of two equal halves of 45 minutes, for a total of 90 minutes of regulation time. That‚Äôs the foundation. Anyone who claims that’s the total time needs to go back to the kids‚Äô league where the clock actually stops.

The most crucial detail to grasp is that the clock on the scoreboard does not stop, regardless of injuries, substitutions, or goal celebrations. This is the single biggest difference between soccer and sports like basketball or American football. This constant-running clock is a key element of the game’s flow, but it’s also the root of all scheduling frustration.

  • The Unseen Clock: The match official (the referee) is the only person who maintains the actual game time.   • A Technicality That Matters: If the referee blows the whistle when the scoreboard hits 45:00, they have made a critical error. The half can only end when the referee determines that all lost time has been played.

The 90 minutes of “regulation” is a mere technicality. Think of it as the base ingredient in a wildly varying recipe‚Äîit‚Äôs the start, not the finish.

The Mandatory Mid-Point: Why Half-Time is Exactly 15 Minutes

The brief interval between the first and second half is called half-time, and its duration is one of the few things in the game that is strictly and consistently enforced. Unless the competition rules specifically state otherwise (e.g., a youth league might use 10 minutes), the interval must not exceed 15 minutes. This is mandated by Law 7.

This isn’t just a time for players to swap jerseys and have a sip of a sports drink. It‚Äôs a vital tactical reset. This fixed 15-minute break allows:

  • Coaching Adjustments: The time where the manager, now seeing the opposition’s true formation and intensity, makes the necessary tactical changes (e.g., shifting from a 4-3-3 to a 4-4-2, or instructing a winger to push up higher).
  • Physical Recovery: Players get crucial rehydration and attention from medical staff, ensuring they can safely go hard for the second 45 minutes.

Don’t be fooled by the simplicity. This 15-minute break is non-negotiable and nearly always adhered to. So, if you’re trying to calculate the total time, the most predictable element is the 15 minutes that separate the two halves.

The Wild Card: Calculating Stoppage Time (Injury Time)

Here is where the game turns into a chaotic mathematical equation, and it‚Äôs what truly answers the question of how long a soccer game lasts. Stoppage time (or “injury time”) is the single most unpredictable element. It‚Äôs the time added to the end of each 45-minute half to compensate for all the time the ball was not in play. The referee, using that secret wristwatch, must account for lost time from these mandatory factors:

  • Injuries/Medical Assessments: Time taken for an injured player to be treated or removed from the field.
  • Disciplinary Sanctions: Time lost while the referee issues yellow or red cards.
  • Goal Celebrations: A surprisingly significant time-eater.

Expertise Signal: Post-2022 FIFA directives actually cracked down on this, instructing referees to be far more rigorous in accounting for lost time, especially goal celebrations. The Final Showdown: Understanding Extra Time and Penalties

If the game requires a winner (such as in a knockout tournament, not a league match), the clock gets extended again, but this time under a different structure: Extra Time (often called “Overtime”). This is not “more stoppage time.” It‚Äôs an entirely new, mandatory segment of play.

When a match goes to Extra Time, the sequence is as follows:

  1. A Second Interval: After the 90 minutes plus stoppage time ends, there is a short break (usually 5 minutes, but sometimes less) before Extra Time starts.
  2. Two 15-Minute Halves: Extra Time consists of two additional halves of 15 minutes each, for a total of 30 extra minutes of mandated play.
  3. Stoppage Time Returns: Crucially, each of these 15-minute periods also has its own dedicated stoppage time added by the referee, just like in regulation.

Data to Consider: A match that goes through 90 minutes, a 15-minute half-time, 5 minutes of second-half stoppage, a 5-minute break, 30 minutes of Extra Time, and another 3 minutes of Extra Time stoppage could easily run for 135 minutes of total elapsed time before the final whistle.

If the score remains tied after Extra Time, the match moves to a Penalty Shootout. This is not a continuation of the match; it is purely a tie-breaking procedure. The shootout itself usually takes an additional 10-15 minutes, depending on how many rounds of penalties are needed to determine the victor.

Therefore, for a knockout game, you should budget at least 2 hours and 20 minutes from the initial kickoff to the final penalty shot.

45 Minutes: The Unstoppable Clock (Why Soccer is Different)

This is the key detail that separates football (soccer) from virtually every other major sport: the game clock counts up and never stops.

In American football or basketball, every incomplete pass, foul, or injury results in a clock stoppage‚Äîthe game itself pauses, and the total elapsed time can stretch well beyond the scheduled window. In soccer, the 45-minute count is an absolute constant, regardless of what happens on the pitch.

What this creates is a distinct, agonizing tension in the final moments of each half. When the scoreboard ticks over to 45:00, the game isn’t over. Instead, the referee signals how much minimum time has been added to compensate for all the time-wasting, substitutions, and injuries that occurred while the clock was still running. This continuous countdown forces players to manage the clock in real-time, knowing every second spent arguing with an official will be paid back with interest later.

The Mandatory 15-Minute Half-Time Interval Rule

The only true, guaranteed stop in regulation play is the mandated half-time interval. This break is exactly 15 minutes maximum, per Law 7 of the IFAB (International Football Association Board) Laws of the Game. Don’t worry, the coaches and players aren’t having a tea party.

The purpose of this 15-minute window is three-fold:

  1. Player Rest: A brief physical recovery after an intense 45 minutes of non-stop running.
  2. Coaching Adjustments: The time where managers make tactical changes, often leading to halftime substitutions that shift the entire dynamic of the second half.
  3. The Media Break: This is the commercial reality. Broadcasters and sponsors rely on this guaranteed window for their breaks, cementing the interval as a non-negotiable part of the professional game.

This 15-minute rule provides a much-needed reset, which contrasts sharply with what happens if the game progresses to Extra Time. If a match needs an additional 30 minutes (two 15-minute periods), the break between the two Extra Time halves is often just one minute‚Äîbarely enough time for a drink and a shout from the coach, proving the 15-minute half-time is more of a strategic pause than a quick breather.

Stoppage Time Is the Wildcard: Why Your Soccer Game Is Never 90 Minutes

The Seven Sins: What Actually Pauses Play (Ref’s Checklist)

The question “How is stoppage time calculated?” is answered by the Laws of the Game. Specifically, IFAB Law 7 gives the referee a mandate, not a suggestion, to account for every second the ball wasn’t in play due to specific incidents. If a referee waves away legitimate time-stoppages, they are neglecting their duty. This isn’t some arbitrary guess; it’s a running checklist of time-sucking events.

The specific factors a referee must account for are:

  • Substitutions: A minimum of 30 seconds is generally awarded for each set of player changes.
  • Assessment/Removal of Injured Players: Time is added for medical staff to enter the pitch and remove a player.
  • Time Wasting: Deliberate delays by players, such as holding the ball on a throw-in.
  • Disciplinary Sanctions: Issuing yellow and red cards, which can take a surprising amount of time.
  • Goal Celebrations: Yes, the joy itself is a time-waster, and rightly so.
  • VAR Checks and Reviews: The time spent by the referee consulting the Video Assistant Referee.
  • Any Other Cause: Anything else that genuinely delays the restart of play.

A classic example of how quickly this compounds is the goal celebration. Knockout Soccer: The Bonus 30 Minutes of Extra Time (and Penalties)

If the competition demands a winner (hello, World Cup and Champions League knockout stages), the unpredictability factor skyrockets. The Rules of Extra Time (ET): When and How Long

Extra Time (ET)‚Äîor ‚ÄúOvertime,‚Äù if you‚Äôre speaking American‚Äîis the mandatory, decisive phase that takes over when a score is tied in an elimination fixture, like a cup final or a tournament knockout round. This is where we must distinguish between the two types of added minutes. Stoppage Time (or Added Time) is simply the referee‚Äôs compensation for in-game delays within the 90 minutes. Extra Time is a structural addition to the match time itself.

The duration is fixed: two additional 15-minute periods. This brings the potential total of competitive play time to 120 minutes. Critically, there is a one-minute pause between the two 15-minute Extra Time halves where players simply switch ends‚Äîthere is no 15-minute half-time break. The referee will also still add Stoppage Time to the end of each 15-minute ET period to account for injuries, goals, or time-wasting during the extension. Unlike the controversial “Golden Goal” or “Silver Goal” formats of the past, teams must play the full 30 minutes unless otherwise specified by a specific tournament’s rules (which is rare in major competitions now).

The Deciding Drama: Penalty Shootouts

What happens if the 120 minutes of play (plus all the respective Stoppage Time) ends and the teams are still deadlocked? The game proceeds directly to a penalty shootout, the definitive, non-gameplay tie-breaker method. There is no re-match, and no more playing time.

The penalty shootout is a best-of-five contest between the teams. Each side selects five players who take alternate kicks against the opposing goalkeeper from the penalty spot.

  • If one team establishes an insurmountable lead before the five kicks are taken (e.g., they are up 3-0 after the third kick, meaning the opponent can only score two more), the shootout ends immediately.
  • If the score is level after the initial five kicks, the shootout enters sudden death. This means both teams continue to take kicks, one at a time, until one team scores and the other misses in the same round.

This process builds trust because it is the final, written-in-stone method for determining a winner. There is no ambiguity; the winner is decided, often in a spectacle of raw pressure and individual skill that has launched‚Äîand ended‚Äîsome of the biggest careers in the sport. It’s an emotionally brutal, but rule-compliant, end to any tied match.
