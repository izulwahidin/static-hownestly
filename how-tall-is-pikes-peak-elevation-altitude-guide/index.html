Decoding the 14,115 ft. Number: USGS and the Datum Debate

The official, current number is 14,115 feet (4,302 meters). You can credit the experts at the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) for that one.

They use incredibly precise surveying tools and a system called a “datum.” Think of a datum like the ruler you use to measure. It’s the fixed point they measure everything else against. The technology they use keeps getting better. That’s why the number sometimes inches up or down over the decades.

The real eye-opener isn’t the final number, but the mountain‚Äôs sheer prominence. That’s how high it is compared to the land right next to it. Pikes Peak shoots up over 5,500 feet from the surrounding plains. That‚Äôs why it looks so massive from Colorado Springs.

So you get to the top and you see a sign that says 14,110 feet. Annoying, I know. It’s like finding a typo on a map.

Here‚Äôs the deal: The elevation has been re-measured many times. Back in 1963, when they built the old Summit House, the number they landed on was 14,110 feet. That number stuck around for decades.

Plus, those early measurements were a little wonky. Sometimes they measured to the highest bit of rock. Other times, they measured to the top of an old lookout tower someone built on the summit. This led to conflicting numbers that were all over the place.
