üõë Why the “2,000-Word Rule” is Total Garbage

Let‚Äôs be honest. The advice to “just make it longer” usually comes from people who want to look smart or who are pushing content mills. They focus on an easy-to-measure number (words) instead of the stuff that‚Äôs hard to measure (reader satisfaction).

  • Quick reality check: If someone spends 10 seconds on your 2,000-word post and then goes back to Google, that tells Google your answer sucked.

You’re basically training your readers to look elsewhere. You’re not writing a novel; you’re writing helpful content. Keep it tight.

Google Cares About Completeness, Not Length

The whole idea that Google rewards sheer volume is outdated. The algorithm today is smart. It understands topics and concepts.

Think about it like this: Google is trying to find the single best answer to a searcher’s question.

  • If the question is “What is a compound sentence?” you need 500 words, tops. You explain it, give examples, and you’re done.
  • If the question is “Comprehensive guide to investing for beginners,” you need to cover stocks, bonds, 401ks, Roth IRAs, and risk tolerance. That naturally needs a lot more words.

The length isn’t the goal; the topic coverage is. You need to be complete enough to satisfy the search, but not so long that you bore the person into clicking away.

üîé How to Find Your REAL Target Word Count (It’s Not a Guessing Game)

So, if we ditch the arbitrary 2,000-word rule, how do you know how much to write? The secret is simple: Look at what‚Äôs already winning.

This is a hot tip from the Department of Obvious, but too many people skip it. You have to peek at the competition.

1. Check the SERP Top 5

  • Scan the titles and H2s: What topics did they cover? Did they talk about the “pros and cons?” Did they include a beginner’s section?
  • Look at the depth: Do they just list 10 things, or do they give three detailed paragraphs for each point?
  • Estimate the word count: You don’t need a fancy tool. Just look at the scrollbar. If the page is endless, you know you’re in the 3,000+ range. If it‚Äôs a quick scroll, you‚Äôre probably under 1,000.

Your goal isn’t to copy them word-for-word, but to figure out the scope of the answer. If the top five articles are all 3,500 words, you need to write at least that much to be comprehensive. If they are all 800 words, writing 3,000 is overkill.

2. Identify the “Missing Piece”

Here’s where you beat the competition and show off your expertise (E-E-A-T, baby!). The top posts are good, but they probably aren’t perfect. Find the gap.

  • Did they forget to mention the cost?
  • Did they skip the part about setting it up on a Mac vs. a PC?
  • Did they offer generic advice instead of a specific case study?

You don’t just write 2,000 words. You write enough words to cover everything they did, plus the one thing they missed. That one unique angle is your ticket to the top.

3. Let the Subheadings Guide You

Instead of counting words, start by outlining your subheadings (H2s and H3s). This is your content map.

If your outline has five big H2 sections and three detailed H3s under each, that structure is going to naturally demand a high word count, maybe 2,500 to 3,000 words.

If your outline is just three simple H2s‚Äî”What It Is,” “How to Do It,” and “One Quick Tip”‚Äîyou might land closer to 800 words. And that’s totally fine!
