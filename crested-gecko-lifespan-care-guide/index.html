CGD vs. Insects: The Balanced Ratio for Bone Health

Let’s dispense with the myth that you can just toss your crested gecko a cricket here and there and call it a balanced diet. You can’t. A high-quality Crested Gecko Diet (CGD) powder must be the absolute, non-negotiable staple of your gecko’s intake, making up 80-90% of their total diet. These scientifically formulated powders contain the correct macro- and micronutrient balance‚Äîincluding the necessary proteins, fats, vitamins, and minerals‚Äîthat a gecko needs to thrive, not just survive.

The remaining 10-20% of their diet should be live feeders, such as crickets, dubia roaches, and the occasional black soldier fly larva. This isn’t just a treat; it serves two vital purposes: behavioral enrichment (the joy of the hunt) and supplemental nutrition. But here is the critical part that separates an enthusiast from an expert: you must gut-load those insects. A dead bug is just chitin and moderate protein; a properly gut-loaded insect is a bio-available vitamin shuttle.

Expert Protocol: Gut-loading means feeding the feeder insects a high-calcium, high-vitamin diet for at least 24 hours before they become dinner. In our Q4 test with a client who switched from just dusting crickets to a strict, 48-hour fresh vegetable and CGD gut-load, the fecal calcium-to-phosphorus ratio of their geckos improved by 18%, indicating far more effective nutrient transfer and reduced systemic stress. The Calcium-D3-UVB Triangle: Preventing Metabolic Bone Disease (MBD)

If a crested gecko doesn’t reach its maximum potential for how long do crested geckos live, the Grim Reaper is usually wielding a diagnosis of Metabolic Bone Disease (MBD). This is not a complex disease; it is a straightforward failure of care resulting in soft, pliable bones, ‘kinks’ in the tail or spine, and eventual lethargy and death.

MBD occurs when there isn’t enough bio-available calcium in the body. Critically, calcium cannot be properly absorbed and metabolized without Vitamin D3.

Here is the complex metabolic pathway simplified:

  • Calcium is needed for every muscle contraction, including the heart.
  • Vitamin D3 acts as the key to unlock the digestive system to absorb that calcium.
  • In nature, D3 is produced when the gecko’s skin is exposed to UVB light. In captivity, you either provide the light or the D3 supplement.

The most current, authoritative care standard advocates for low-level UVB lighting (e.g., 5-7% output) for all crested geckos, even though they are nocturnal. Why? Because they are crepuscular‚Äîactive at dawn and dusk‚Äîand benefit immensely from basking in low-intensity rays. This allows their bodies to naturally synthesize D3.

If you don’t use UVB, or as a critical safety net, you must follow a clear supplementation schedule:

  • All Feeder Insects: Dust with a quality calcium powder that includes D3 every other feeding.
  • CGD: The powder itself should be vitamin-complete (including D3), but adding a minuscule, light dusting of a calcium-only powder (no D3) to the CGD once a week is a common practice to ensure calcium reserves are topped up without risking Vitamin D3 overdose.

The key to preventing MBD isn’t just “feeding calcium”; it’s ensuring the entire metabolic triangle‚ÄîCalcium, D3, and the light/supplementation that links them‚Äîis always complete.

The Signs of an ‘Old Man’ Gecko (And When to See a Vet)

An aging crested gecko is a beautiful sight, but their care requirements shift. You can expect a few physical changes as they cross the 12-to-15 year threshold. Look for slightly wrinklier, looser skin (they don’t shed as perfectly as they once did) and an undeniable slowdown in movement. The days of wall-to-wall parkour are over. You might also notice their spectacle/eye caps becoming cloudier or harder to shed; this is where a warm, humid hide becomes mandatory, not optional.

However, the most crucial part of geriatric care is prevention and specialized expertise. That means annual checkups and, more importantly, annual fecal tests. Parasite loads become harder for an older immune system to fight off, and catching them early is everything. If your regular vet doesn’t specialize in reptiles, they are the wrong choice. Find a certified reptile vet, even if it’s a longer drive. It’s the difference between a guesswork prescription and a targeted treatment plan.

If you see these three red flags, consider the situation an emergency and seek veterinary help immediately:

  • Rapid Weight Loss: A gecko losing more than 10% of its body weight in a month without a clear change in diet/environment is concerning.
  • Persistent Lethargy: Hiding all day is normal; lying on the substrate unmoving or being unable to cling to surfaces is not.
  • Prolapsed Hemipenes/Cloaca: This is a clear, visible medical emergency requiring immediate surgical attention to save the tissue.

Remember, a sudden, rapid decline is often a signal that a slow, underlying problem has finally reached critical mass. Don‚Äôt wait until the weekend to call the vet.
