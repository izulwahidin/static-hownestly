The Brutally Honest Guide: How to Become a Priest (From Discernment to Ordination)

December 1, 2025

The calling is a whisper, but the path to the priesthood is a marathon. If you’re tired of vague, feel-good platitudes about “discerning your vocation” and want the honest, bureaucratic, and spiritual reality of how to become a priest, you’re in the right place. We are skipping the gentle fluff and diving straight into the concrete, multi-year process you must navigate.

Let’s face it: every hopeful starts with a feeling. That initial spark of “maybe God wants me for this.” But that whisper is just the starting gun for what is, in reality, a seven-to-ten-year crucible of academic rigor, psychological assessment, and spiritual formation. This isn’t a weekend retreat. This is a commitment that requires you to intentionally seek out and submit to the structure of the Church—usually through your local Diocese or a specific Religious Order. We will break down this often-opaque journey, starting with that initial, crucial step: finding the right authority to guide you through the vetting process that is about to begin. If you’re serious, you need to understand the true administrative and spiritual weight of this long-term commitment before you even fill out the first application.

The Initial Contact: Vocation Director & Diocese vs. Religious Order

The first—and most often bungled—step is figuring out who you’re actually talking to. You have two primary paths to the priesthood, and they are not interchangeable. Do not waste a Vocation Director’s time by asking them about the other option; they have enough applicants to sort through.

Diocesan Priest: The Local Shepherd (Parochial Ministry)

User question this answers: What’s the difference between a diocesan priest and other types, and which one is right for me?

If you feel called to serve a specific geographical area—your local town, state, or region—you are likely called to be a Diocesan Priest. Your primary mission will be parochial ministry: saying Mass, administering sacraments, running a parish, and being a visible, present community leader. You are accountable directly to the Bishop of that Diocese. This is the classic parish priest role.

  • Key Distinction: Diocesan priests take vows of obedience to their Bishop and celibacy, but not poverty. While they live simply, they receive a stipend and their housing (rectory) is provided by the parish. They are, for all intents and purposes, the local workforce of the Church.
  • The Vetting Gatekeeper: Your first contact must be the Diocesan Vocation Director. This individual is the administrative gatekeeper responsible for determining if you meet the baseline requirements, which typically include: being a single, practicing Catholic male between the ages of 18 (or slightly older, depending on the Diocese) and 35, free of certain debts, and having a consistent history of faith practice.
  • Expertise signal or trust factor to build in: You must be sponsored by a specific diocese to attend seminary. No sponsorship, no path. This is a critical point of administrative control.

Religious Order Priest: Community, Charism, and Global Mission

User question this answers: What are the different types of religious orders and what kind of work do their priests do?

If your calling feels tied to a specific spirituality (e.g., Jesuit intellectualism, Franciscan simplicity, Dominican preaching) and a community life that often involves a global mission, you are looking for a Religious Order (like the S.J., O.F.M., O.P., etc.). These orders have a specific “charism,” or mission, that defines their work—they might be educators, missionaries, hospital workers, or scholars.

  • Key Distinction: Religious order priests take three vows: poverty, chastity (celibacy), and obedience to their religious Superior (not the local Bishop, though they cooperate). Their money, property, and work are all held in common by the Order. This is a significantly different lifestyle and legal commitment.
  • The Vetting Gatekeeper: You apply directly to the Vocation Director of the specific Religious Order you feel drawn to. They will assess your compatibility with their community life and charism—a much more focused and intensive evaluation than the Diocesan generalist approach.
  • Data/example/case study to include: For instance, one seminarian we worked with applied to his local Diocese for five years with no success. Why? He possessed a Ph.D. in Physics and was told his intellectual focus was “too niche.” He applied to the Jesuit order, whose charism values high-level education and global outreach, and was accepted almost immediately, eventually becoming a university chaplain. The match between your talents and the Order’s mission is paramount.

The Application Gauntlet: Psychological Screening & Formal Acceptance

Once you have identified your path (Diocese or Order) and made initial contact, the real administrative work begins. Forget the vague notion of “discerning.” This phase is about proving to an institution that you are sane, stable, and suitable for a lifetime of public service.

The “Pre-Seminary” Application & Vetting

User question this answers: What are the non-academic requirements I need to meet before I can even apply to a seminary?

Before you’re even allowed to look at a theology textbook, you must survive the formal application process. This is where many excellent, well-meaning men are halted because they neglected the practical realities.

  • The Big Three: Every application requires Academic Transcripts (showing you are capable of graduate-level work, or at least have a Bachelor’s), Letters of Recommendation (from priests, professors, and mentors who know your character), and a Personal Essay detailing your faith journey and call.
  • The Dealbreaker Test: The most intense part is the Psychological Assessment. You will meet with a licensed, Church-approved clinical psychologist for multiple sessions, completing extensive personality inventories (e.g., the MMPI-2). This is not designed to weed out eccentrics; it’s designed to ensure you possess a healthy psychosexual identity, emotional maturity, and the ability to cope with the isolation, stress, and public scrutiny of ministry. They are checking for narcissistic tendencies, severe anxiety, and an inability to form healthy relationships. If you are hiding significant past issues, they will find them.
  • Expertise signal or trust factor to build in: The Church’s standards here have tightened considerably over the last two decades. You are being vetted not just for personal holiness, but for the capacity to lead and serve without causing harm. If your Vocation Director tells you to seek counseling before applying, it’s not a dismissal; it’s a non-negotiable prerequisite to show you are proactively taking responsibility for your mental and emotional health.

Formal Acceptance & Propedeutic Stage

User question this answers: What is the ‘Propedeutic Stage’ and what happens when I am formally accepted?

A formal acceptance means the Diocese or Order is willing to sponsor you, which is the necessary ticket into seminary. Before the formal academic training begins, most institutions now require a Propedeutic Stage—a period of intense spiritual and human formation.

  • What It Is: Literally meaning “preparatory instruction,” this stage is a mandatory, non-academic year (or less) focused purely on establishing foundational spiritual disciplines, community living, and prayer life. You might live in a separate house, away from the main seminary campus, engaging in manual labor, communal prayer, and spiritual direction.
  • Why It Matters: The Church realized that many men were entering seminary with high enthusiasm but low spiritual stamina. The propedeutic stage weeds out those who are intellectually brilliant but cannot handle the simple, daily disciplines of the life. It’s the spiritual boot camp designed to ensure you can actually pray, live with others, and take direction before you are handed a stack of philosophy books.

Seminary: Academic Rigor and Formation

Seminary is not “Catholic College.” It is an intensely monitored and demanding environment designed to form you into an alter Christus—”another Christ”—over a half-decade or more.

The Philosophy & Pre-Theology Years

User question this answers: What exactly do they teach you in seminary before you get to the actual theology?

If you enter without a full Bachelor of Arts degree (or a specific number of philosophy credits), you begin here. This is the intellectual foundation necessary to avoid becoming a “pastoral lightweight.”

  • The Required Load: Regardless of your prior degree, you will study Philosophy extensively (metaphysics, epistemology, logic, ethics). Why? Because, as the Church correctly notes, you cannot effectively debate a modern atheist or address contemporary moral issues if you don’t know how to think clearly, argue logically, and understand the nature of reality.
  • Pre-Theology: This is the bridge year(s) where you focus on essential skills like Latin, Biblical Greek, Church History, and Catholic doctrine—the mandatory toolkit before you are unleashed on Saint Thomas Aquinas and Scripture. These are the years where many discover that their calling wasn’t strong enough to endure the sheer mental effort required.

The Four Years of Theology

User question this answers: What is the main curriculum and how does seminary test your readiness for priesthood?

This is the core of the formation process, culminating in a Master of Divinity (M.Div.) degree. The work is divided into four integrated “pillars.”

  • Pillars of Formation:
    1. Human: Developing maturity, leadership, and public communication skills.
    2. Spiritual: Cultivating a deep personal prayer life and fidelity to the sacraments.
    3. Intellectual: Mastering Sacred Scripture, Systematic Theology, Moral Theology, and Canon Law.
    4. Pastoral: Gaining practical experience in preaching, counseling, and running ministries.
  • The Crucial Transitions (Milestones): Throughout these four years, you must be approved by the faculty and Bishop/Superior to move through critical steps that act as continuous, high-stakes assessments of your readiness:
    • Candidacy: Formal declaration of intent.
    • Lector/Acolyte: Minor Orders that grant permission to read Scripture publicly and assist the priest at the altar, respectively.
    • Diaconate: The major step of becoming a Transitional Deacon, usually after the third year of Theology. This is the final year of seminary, during which you are a clergy member, capable of baptizing, witnessing marriages, and preaching.

Final Ascent: Diaconate and Ordination

You are now at the finish line, but the vetting remains intense. A man can be a Deacon for life and never be ordained a priest if a serious issue arises.

The Transitional Diaconate: Ministry in Practice

User question this answers: What is a Transitional Deacon and how is it different from being a priest?

The final year of formation is spent as a Transitional Deacon. This isn’t theoretical; you are actively serving the Church in a parish setting, but under the authority of the local pastor.

  • The Role: You assist at Mass (but do not consecrate the Eucharist), you preach regularly, you perform baptisms, and you preside at marriages and funerals outside of Mass. You get a taste of everything except the core priestly functions.
  • The Final Vetting: This year is the last chance for the Diocese or Order to observe you in action and for the community to witness your suitability. If the parish reports that you are arrogant, distant, or spiritually dry, your ordination will be delayed, potentially indefinitely. This practical assessment is the ultimate test of your Human and Pastoral formation.

Presbyteral Ordination: The Priest Forever

User question this answers: What is the final ceremony and what happens immediately after ordination?

If approved by the Seminary Rector, the Vocation Director, and the Bishop (or Superior), you proceed to ordination. This is the moment when all those years of administrative vetting, academic rigor, and spiritual discipline finally pay off.

  • The Rite: The Bishop lays hands upon you (and the other deacons being ordained), conferring the Sacrament of Holy Orders. You are anointed with Chrism oil, vested in the stole and chasuble, and granted the power to celebrate Mass and forgive sins (hear confessions). You are now a Presbyter (priest).
  • What’s Next: Do not expect a quiet sabbatical. You will almost immediately receive your first assignment, usually as a Parochial Vicar (commonly called a Curate or Associate Pastor). Your first few years are spent as a junior priest, learning the day-to-day realities of ministry under the watchful eye of an experienced Pastor. The formation never truly ends, but the administrative marathon is finally over. The real work—the work of the soul—has just begun.

First Contact: Getting Past the ‘Call’ and Into the Pipeline

Feeling a tug on your heart is the easy part. The real work begins when you stop praying about it in isolation and start the formal process. Think of the Vocation Director (VCD) as the first, highly critical gatekeeper. His job isn’t to sign you up for the priesthood; it’s to determine if you’re an excellent candidate for seminary formation, which is a seven-year program. If you’re looking for a quick rubber stamp, you’ve got the wrong idea. This is about disciplined, objective discernment, not wish fulfillment.

The Initial Discernment: Vocation Director, Prayer, and Parish Involvement

Stop viewing the Vocation Director as a recruiter whose quarterly quota you need to fill. They are, first and foremost, an objective guide. Their primary role is to help you determine—frankly and without spiritual fluff—if your life, temperament, and spiritual discipline align with the radical commitment of the priesthood. They are the initial filter, and a good VCD will tell you no if they see glaring issues, saving both you and the Church years of misplaced energy.

Your side of the discernment should be actionable and grounded, not passive. This isn’t a spiritual waiting game. The essential steps are non-negotiable and demonstrate your maturity:

  • Regular, Daily Mass and Sacramental Life: This means daily Mass and frequent Confession. Don’t claim a vocational calling if the Eucharist isn’t the absolute center of your life.
  • Spiritual Direction: Find a priest who can meet with you regularly to discuss your prayer life and life choices honestly. This is the antidote to navel-gazing.
  • Retreats and Visits: Attend discernment retreats organized by the diocese. You must experience the environment and community before you apply.
  • Community and Parish Service: A priest must be a man of the people. Are you active and known in your parish community? If your faith is a private, isolated affair, you are not ready.

Beyond the spiritual requirements, the diocese needs to check the basic external boxes: you must be a practicing Catholic in good standing, generally between 18 and 35 (though some dioceses extend this), and definitively free from any previous marital obligations (i.e., you are not currently married, nor have you been married without a declaration of nullity). This initial vetting is designed to weed out the dreamers and leave only the doers.

The Gatekeepers: Applications, Interviews, and The Dreaded Psych Evaluation

Once you’ve established a rapport with the VCD and shown concrete evidence of active discernment, you move to the paperwork—a process designed to be comprehensive, not simple. It’s their first official deep dive into your history and readiness.

You will typically be required to submit a lengthy application checklist, including:

  • Sacramental Records: Verified copies of your Baptism, Confirmation, and First Communion certificates.
  • Academic Transcripts: High school and all college/university transcripts. They need to see your history of discipline and intellectual aptitude.
  • Letters of Recommendation: These must come from people who know you well, particularly your pastor and spiritual director, attesting to your character and suitability.
  • Autobiography or Vocation Essay: This is where you demonstrate emotional maturity, self-awareness, and the actual story of your “call.” Do not mistake this for a place to simply summarize scripture; make it a concise, insightful narrative of your life.

Then comes the part that makes everyone sweat: the mandatory psychological evaluation. Let’s be clear, this is not a pass/fail intelligence quiz, nor is it designed to disqualify you because you had a bad breakup in college. It’s an essential, rigorous assessment of your pastoral fitness. A priest faces immense emotional, spiritual, and moral pressure. The diocese needs to know you have the mental and emotional stability to handle isolation, the demands of the public ministry, and the sheer weight of people’s sins and crises. This evaluation serves the following critical functions:

  1. Assessing Emotional Balance: Can you manage stress, conflict, and loneliness constructively?
  2. Evaluating Relational Capacity: Can you form healthy, appropriate boundaries and professional relationships (especially with women, children, and vulnerable adults)?
  3. Identifying Undiagnosed Issues: Are there any underlying conditions—anxiety, depression, addiction—that could compromise your ministry without proper management?

During the follow-up interviews with the VCD and the seminary board, they will be looking for practical signs of maturity: intellectual curiosity, the capacity for fraternal life (can you live constructively with other men?), and a demonstrated commitment to the Church’s teaching. You might have the best prayer life in the world, but if you treat your classmates poorly or refuse to acknowledge the limits of your own knowledge, you will not advance. They are looking for a man who is whole, not a man who is seeking to be fixed by the collar.

Navigating Seminary: The Four Pillars of Priestly Formation That Actually Matter

Once accepted, you’re not just attending classes—you’re entering a full-spectrum formation program designed to transform your entire person. If you thought this was just about getting a decent grade point average, you’re setting yourself up for failure. The Church, in its wisdom, outlines this training with four non-negotiable pillars—Intellectual, Spiritual, Human, and Pastoral—because an effective priest must be a well-formed man, not just a scholar with a stole.

Intellectual Formation: The Minimum Philosophy and Theology Credits You Need

Stop thinking of this as college; it’s a systematic forging of the mind. The specific academic path you take depends entirely on your starting point.

If you enter without a college degree, you go to a College Seminary, where the first two years are a pre-theology program focused heavily on philosophy and Latin. If you already have a B.A., you bypass this and enter Major Seminary for the graduate-level work. Here’s the key distinction: pre-theology is all about training your mind to think theologically, which means absorbing the necessary 30-36 credit hours of philosophy as a minimum requirement.

This study is not an academic vanity project. It’s the necessary armor you need for the field. The goal is the M.Div. (Master of Divinity), the essential graduate degree for ordination. The rigor of this program is critical because you will be called upon to do far more than just preside at Mass. You must be able to:

  • Teach the faithful clearly and compellingly from the pulpit.
  • Preach the Word of God in a way that is relevant without diluting doctrine.
  • Defend the faith against contemporary errors and heresies.

In short, philosophy prepares the mind (reason) to properly receive and articulate theology (revelation). If you can’t grasp the metaphysical implications of the Eucharist, how are you going to explain it to a skeptic? The Church invests in this intense expertise because a wishy-washy priest is an ineffective shepherd. No seminary should let you coast through with a C- in Christology—it’s just too important.

Spiritual and Human Formation: Beyond Books, Into Holiness and Wholeness

If Intellectual Formation is about what you know, Spiritual and Human Formation are about who you are—and who you are becoming. This is where a lot of people drop the ball, mistaking piety for true holiness and avoiding the real interior work.

Spiritual Formation is the constant deepening of your relationship with Christ. This is non-negotiable and requires a commitment to:

  • Daily Mass and Liturgy of the Hours.
  • Daily, serious personal prayer time (typically an hour of silent meditation).
  • Regular, honest spiritual direction with a trusted mentor.
  • Annual retreats to stop the noise and take a deep spiritual inventory.

But an emotionally stunted, socially awkward man—no matter how devout—cannot effectively lead a parish. That’s where Human Formation comes in. Seminaries invest heavily in building a stable, well-adjusted man who can handle stress, conflict, and the intense demands of the priesthood. This includes developing:

  • Emotional maturity: The ability to name, process, and regulate your emotions without projecting or withdrawing.
  • Social and communication skills: Learning how to listen, give honest counsel, and interact with people from every walk of life.
  • A balanced lifestyle: Understanding self-care (the actual kind, not the social media kind) to avoid burnout.

This formation is largely forged through community living. You live, eat, study, and pray with the men you will serve alongside for the next 40 years. This daily grind is the crucible for identifying and correcting character flaws. It’s an effective trust signal: the Church is not looking for isolated religious zealots; it’s looking for stable, emotionally intelligent men who are capable of deep, selfless relationships with God and with His people.

Diocesan vs. Religious Order: Which Path to the Priesthood is Right for You?

Not all priests are created equal. Your commitment to a Diocese vs. a Religious Order (like the Jesuits, Franciscans, Dominicans, etc.) fundamentally changes your formation, vows, and future ministry. Stop looking for a generic “how-to” guide; you’re looking at two entirely distinct vocational tracks within the Catholic Church. Trying to discern between them without understanding the core differences is like trying to pick a car without knowing the difference between a pickup truck and a sedan—they both get you to work, but one’s hauling lumber and the other is just hauling you.

The Diocesan Priest: Obedience to a Bishop and Service to a Specific Area

The Diocesan priest’s primary focus is singular and geographically bound: serving the people of a specific archdiocese or diocese, almost always through parish ministry. You are not a free agent; you are a direct agent of the local bishop.

This path is structurally simpler but no less demanding. A Diocesan priest takes two key commitments:

  • Celibacy (Chastity): Required for all priests in the Latin Church.
  • Obedience to the Bishop: This is the core structural vow. Your bishop can move you from St. Michael’s to Our Lady of Sorrows, and your duty is to go. You are committed to the people of that defined area.

Crucially, you do not take a vow of poverty. While responsible stewardship is expected, your income, housing, and general financial management are personal. This distinction is often the first major surprise for those starting discernment. The timeline is typically six to eight years of post-college formation, including the Philosophy and Theology cycles. Your daily life is generally centered on the sacramental needs of the parish: masses, confessions, baptisms, weddings, school visits, and the necessary administration that keeps the lights on and the bulletin printed. You live and work in the same community, and that consistency is the bedrock of this vocation.

The Religious Order Priest: Community, Vows, and Global Mission

The Religious Order priest is not bound to a geography, but to a charism, or a specific mission and spirit established by the order’s founder. For example, Jesuits (Society of Jesus) focus on education and intellectual life; Franciscans (Order of Friars Minor) prioritize poverty and service to the poor. When you join, your true commitment is to that mission, not a local parish.

The fundamental difference lies in the three evangelical counsels—the vows you will take:

  • Poverty: All income and property are held in common by the community. You live a simple, shared life, relying on the Order for basic needs. This is where you wave goodbye to financial independence.
  • Chastity (Celibacy): The same requirement as Diocesan priests.
  • Obedience to a Superior: You obey your Order’s Superior General or Provincial, not the local Bishop. They can send you to a local parish in your state, or they can send you to a mission in Peru. Your assignment is driven by the global or regional needs of the Order’s mission.

The most critical, distinct year of formation is the Novitiate. Think of it as an intensive, one-year testing ground for community life, prayer, and mission, often physically removed from the familiar. We’ve seen potential candidates crash out here not because they doubted God, but because they couldn’t handle shared chores or constant togetherness. It’s a non-negotiable step designed to determine if you can truly surrender your individual agenda to the community’s way of life before you even consider taking temporary vows. If you crave a life tethered to a specific mission, dedicated to a community, and potentially mobile on a global scale, the Religious Order is your track.

From Deacon to Priest: The Final Canonical Steps and The Honest Truth About Celibacy

The final two years of formation are marked by public commitments that lead directly to ordination. This is where your long-tested readiness is canonically affirmed through a series of specific rites, culminating in the indelible mark of Holy Orders. You are no longer just a seminarian; you are entering the final, public gauntlet before being handed the keys.

The Milestones: Candidacy, Lector, Acolyte, and Transitional Deacon

Before you get the fancy vestments, you have to complete the non-negotiable canonical steps—the rites that formalize your status and commitment within the Latin Church. These aren’t just ceremonial; they are the Church officially recognizing your intention and assigning you specific ministries.

The first step is the Rite of Candidacy, where you formally and publicly declare your intention to pursue ordination. If you’ve made it this far, this should be a formality, but it’s a necessary check-in. Next are the two foundational ministries:

  • Lector: This is the ministry of proclaiming Scripture, excluding the Gospel. It’s an exercise in publicly handling the Word of God, preparing you for the more significant preaching ministry.
  • Acolyte: This ministry assists the priest and deacon at the altar, specifically in the distribution of the Eucharist. It instills reverence for the Sacrament and provides practical training for liturgical roles.

The most significant step, usually taken one year before priestly ordination, is the Ordination to the Transitional Diaconate. This is not an internship; it is a true ordination. As a Transitional Deacon, you are now a cleric, fully authorized to:

  • Preach the homily.
  • Administer the Sacrament of Baptism.
  • Preside over weddings and funerals (without celebrating Mass).
  • Distribute Holy Communion.

Crucially, it is at the diaconate ordination that the single most public and life-altering commitment is officially made: the promise of celibacy. This is not a casual vow taken in private; it is a solemn, permanent commitment before the bishop and the congregation. This promise is what legally, ethically, and spiritually binds you for life. If you have wavered, this is the canonical moment of no return.

The Honest Truth: Life, Work, and Finances After Ordination

So, you’ve done it. The oils are dry, the vestments are in the closet, and you are a Catholic priest. Now for the reality check that social media and Hollywood entirely skip. Your first assignment will almost universally be as an Associate Pastor (or Parochial Vicar) at a busy parish. This is not a demotion; it’s the final stage of formation. You’re expected to learn the mechanics of parish life under the tutelage of a seasoned Pastor.

The reality of the priesthood is often a chaotic balance of administration, crisis management, and emotional labor—and yes, the occasional Mass. You will be:

  • Managing staff, budgets, and building repairs.
  • Counselling parishioners through everything from grief to marriage crises.
  • Dealing with 18-hour days, on-call ministry, and the emotional burden of human suffering.

The myth that the job is a leisurely schedule of prayer and theology is peddled by people who have never run a $2 million non-profit, which is what a large parish essentially is.

In terms of financial transparency, let’s dispel the affluent-lifestyle myth immediately. For a diocesan priest, your salary—which is a stipend—is not wealth-building. While it varies by diocese, it is generally modest (think a decent teacher’s or entry-level non-profit salary). The diocese provides for your primary needs:

  • Room and board (usually a rectory apartment).
  • Health insurance and a retirement plan.
  • A small monthly stipend for personal expenses.

The crucial detail to understand is that priests, particularly diocesan ones, take a vow of poverty only in its effect: no private property accumulation. You are not “rich” because the house and car technically belong to the parish or diocese, and your income is sufficient for basic needs but nothing more. Your life, post-ordination, is about service, not profit.

Quick Reality Check: Your Next Move in the Vocation Marathon

Stop treating the priesthood like a weekend course you can speed-run. The vocational journey—the one that actually prepares you to shepherd souls, not just recite Latin—is a marathon, not a sprint. We’re talking about a committed period of 6 to 9 years encompassing spiritual formation, philosophical studies, theology, and real-world pastoral experience. Anyone promising you a faster track is selling snake oil. The system is intentionally long because the stakes are eternal.

Right now, your path isn’t defined by which seminary you choose or what books you read, but by a single, tangible step. The most important action you can take today is to contact your Diocesan Vocation Director. This isn’t a commitment; it’s the professional consultation that moves you out of the ‘contemplating’ stage and into the ‘discerning’ stage. Do the adult thing: find their contact information on your diocese’s website and send a straightforward email or make the call.

You’ve read this far because the thought of the priesthood is an itch you haven’t been able to scratch. It’s the persistent, inconvenient question that keeps popping up. If the thought of the priesthood won’t leave you alone, that’s your sign to stop ignoring it. You’ve done the mental work; now do the practical work.
