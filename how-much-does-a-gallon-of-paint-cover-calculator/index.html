üßê The Math That Actually Matters: Forget the “One Size Fits All”

Look, the 350‚Äì400 square feet is your starting point. Think of it like a base speed limit. But you’re almost always going to need two coats for a decent finish. So, for a two-coat job, that single gallon really only covers 175 to 200 square feet total.

üé® Three Things That Will Steal Your Coverage

You can measure your walls perfectly, but if you ignore these three things, you’ll still run out of paint. These are the main culprits that force you to buy extra gallons.

1. The Color and Contrast Game

This is a huge factor people forget. Are you painting a light color over a dark wall? You’re going to need more coats‚Äîperiod.

  • Dark-to-Light Switch: If you’re going from, say, a deep red to a pale gray, that old red is going to fight back. You’ll likely need three coats, or at least a good coat of primer first. Primer only covers about 200‚Äì300 square feet per gallon because it’s designed to soak in and prepare the surface.
  • Light-to-Light: This is the easiest. If you’re just freshening up a cream wall with a slightly different cream, you might actually get away with one coat, or a super-easy two.

Hot take: Don’t buy that “paint-and-primer-in-one” stuff if you’re making a dramatic color change. Just buy a separate can of primer. It’s cheaper and will save you a ton of time and frustration later.

2. Wall Texture and Porosity

What your wall is made of matters a lot. Think of it like trying to paint a piece of smooth glass versus a thick bath towel.

  • Smooth Walls: Standard drywall or previously painted, smooth surfaces let the paint glide on. You get the best coverage here.
  • Textured or Rough Walls: Stucco, brick, or even that weird “popcorn” ceiling texture has a ton more surface area than a smooth wall. The paint soaks into every tiny crack. Plan to buy 20% to 30% more paint than your calculation suggests.
  • Fresh Drywall: Raw, unpainted drywall is a sponge. It will suck the life out of your paint. You absolutely, 100% need to prime it first. Otherwise, you’ll waste two gallons of paint for every one you expected to use.

3. Paint Quality (Stop Being So Cheap!)

I know, I know. That $20-a-gallon paint is tempting. But here’s the secret: the good stuff is worth the money.

  • Premium Paint has more pigment and better binders. These fancy words just mean the color is stronger and it sticks better. It covers those 400 square feet more easily and often looks great in two coats.
  • Economy Paint is mostly water and filler. It‚Äôs thin, the color is weak, and you’ll end up needing three or four coats just to cover the wall evenly. You save $15 on the can, but you end up having to buy two more cans, plus you wasted an extra day painting. Don’t be that person.

üìè How to Be a Paint Coverage Wizard

You don’t need a crazy formula. Just follow this simple plan to know exactly how many gallons of paint cover your project.

  1. Measure Your Space: Find the square footage of every surface you want to paint. Measure the width of a wall, then the height, and multiply the two numbers. Don’t forget the ceiling if you’re painting that, too.
  2. Subtract the Non-Paintable Stuff: Measure doors and windows and subtract their square footage from the total. A standard door is about 20 sq. ft.
  3. Multiply by the Coats: Take your total square footage and multiply it by two (for two coats). If you’re doing a dark-to-light change on textured walls, multiply by three just to be safe.
  4. Divide by the Coverage Rate: Take that final number and divide it by 350 (to be safe, even if your paint says 400).

That final number is how many gallons you need. Round up, always!

Example: A 10-foot by 12-foot room with 8-foot ceilings.

  • Wall area: $2 \times (10 \times 8) + 2 \times (12 \times 8) = 160 + 192 = 352$ sq. ft.
  • Total for two coats: $352 \times 2 = 704$ sq. ft.
  • Gallons needed (dividing by 350): $704 / 350 \approx 2.01$ gallons.

You need 3 gallons to be safe, especially after cutting in the corners. See? Three, not two. That little bit of extra paint is the difference between a panicked drive to the store and a clean-up job.
