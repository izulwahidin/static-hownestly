üõë Never, Ever Use Soap or Oil (Seriously)

Here’s the first and most important rule, so listen up: Don’t use dish soap. Not even a little bit.

Your pizza stone is porous, which is a fancy way of saying it has a million tiny holes. When you use soap, the stone sucks it up like a dry sponge in the desert. Then, the next time you heat it up, all you’ll smell is Ivory or whatever you use. Your pizza will taste like a bubble bath, and nobody wants that. Soap is the enemy!

And please, don’t try to oil it to “season” it, either. That works great for cast iron pans, but for a pizza stone? You’ll just end up with a sticky, smoking mess in your oven. The stone seasons itself naturally from the bits of flour and fat that burn off the pizza. That dark color that forms? That’s your friend!

The takeaway here is simple: Less is more when it comes to washing this thing.

üî• The “Scrape and Burn” Method: Your New Best Friend

So, if you can’t use soap, how do you get the baked-on goo off the stone? You use two things: a scraper and fire (aka your oven).

This is the fastest, easiest, and most effective way to clean your stone. It sounds dramatic, but trust me, it works.

Step 1: Scrape Off the Big Stuff

First, let the stone cool down completely. Don’t try to scrape a super hot stone‚Äîyou’ll burn yourself, and the drastic temperature change could actually crack the stone. We don’t want that kind of drama.

Once it’s cool, use a metal spatula or a plastic scraper to gently scrape off any big bits of stuck-on food. Think of cheese pools or crust pieces. You don’t need to scrub hard. Just loosen the pieces so they flake off.

If you have a really gnarly spot, you can use a nylon brush to scrub it. Just use a little plain hot water on the brush, NO soap, remember?

Step 2: The High-Heat Burn-Off

This is the fun part where you let your oven do the hard work. This process is called pyrolysis, which just means using super high heat to turn gunk into ash.

  1. Heat it up: Put the stone in your oven.
  2. Turn up the heat: Crank the temperature up to the highest setting your oven allows‚Äîusually around 500¬∞F (260¬∞C) or even use the self-cleaning cycle if your oven has one and the stone manufacturer says it’s okay (check their website!).
  3. Wait and watch: Let the stone bake for an hour or two. The intense heat will burn away anything organic‚Äîall those little grease spots and splatters turn into harmless ash.

When you’re done, turn off the oven and let the stone cool down completely again. It needs to be room temperature before you touch it.

Step 3: Wipe Away the Ash

After the stone is cool, you’ll see a fine, white powder left over. That’s the ash from all the burnt gunk! Just take a clean, dry towel or a clean nylon brush and wipe it off.

Your stone might still have some dark spots. Don’t stress about these! Those are the seasoning spots we talked about, and they just mean your stone is working perfectly. A perfectly clean, light-colored pizza stone is actually less effective than a dark, seasoned one.

üöø Quick Reality Check on Water and Storage

Okay, we’ve covered the cleaning, but what about water?

The quick answer is to use as little water as possible. If you need to rinse off some loose crumbs, go for it. But don’t soak the stone. Seriously, don’t let it sit in a sink full of water.

If the stone gets wet, it must be 100% dry before you heat it up again. If there’s still water inside the porous stone, it can turn into steam quickly in your hot oven and crack the stone.

Look, I’ve had a stone crack on me before, and it was a total tragedy. So, if you had to use water, let it air dry for a full day before you put it back in the oven. Even better, put it in a cold oven and heat it up slowly the next time you bake. That’ll guarantee it’s dry inside.

And finally, how to store it? Just leave it in your oven! Most people just keep their stone in the oven all the time. It actually helps keep your oven temperature more stable, making everything you bake‚Äînot just pizza‚Äîturn out better. It’s a win-win.
